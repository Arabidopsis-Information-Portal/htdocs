3. INTRODUCTION

Enhancer traps and gene traps are reporter gene constructs that can respond to cis-acting transcriptional signals when fused to chromosomal DNA. Insertional mutagenesis using these "traps" involves generating a large number of individuals that have the reporter gene integrated at different sites throughout the genome. Their progeny are collected and examined for (i) expression of the reporter gene; and (ii) mutant phenotypes caused by insertion. In lines in which the reporter gene is inserted within or near a chromosomal gene, reporter gene expression mimics that of the chromosomal gene. In the last few years enhancer traps and gene traps have been extensively exploited in Drosophila and in the mouse, leading to the isolation of genes important in development whose phenotypes would have been missed in conventional mutagenesis screens. This is because reporter gene expression patterns can be examined in heterozygous individuals, which should be viable even if the insertion is homozygous lethal. Reporter gene expression patterns can also be informative when the insertion does not result in an obvious mutant phenotype.

This laboratory will introduce you to our enhancer trap and gene trap system in Arabidopsis, which was developed by V. Sundaresan and R. Martienssen at Cold Spring Harbor, in collaboration with Hong Ma (CSHL), Jonathan Jones and Caroline Dean (John Innes Centre, UK). Enhancer trap reporter genes have a minimal promoter that is only expressed when inserted near cis-acting chromosomal enhancers. Gene trap reporter genes (promoter traps and exon traps) have no promoter; reporter gene expression can occur only when the reporter gene inserts within a transcribed chromosomal gene, creating a transcriptional fusion.

Our system uses both enhancer trap and gene trap reporters, and is based on the maize Ac and Ds transposable elements. The Ds elements carry the ß-glucuronidase (uidA) gene as a reporter and the neomycin phosphotransferase (NPTII) gene (conferring resistance to kanamycin) as a selectable marker. There are two classes of Ds elements: (i) an enhancer trap element which has a minimal promoter fused to the uidA gene, and (ii) a gene trap element, which has a multiple splice acceptor fused to the uidA gene. Random insertions of the Ds element throughout the genome allow us to detect chromosomal gene expression through the activation of the uidA gene. We can detect this expression in the absence of a mutant phenotype, so genes performing redundant functions can be identified. We may also be able to assess the role of essential genes, because the GUS expression pattern can be examined in viable heterozygotes.

Ds elements are transactivated by crossing to transgenic plants that provide a source of transposase, namely an immobilized Ac element. Ac and Ds transposable elements have a tendency to transpose to genetically linked locations in the genome approximately 50% of the time, which is a major drawback to using them for random tagging. We have developed a selection scheme which allows us to enrich for unlinked transposition events, by selecting against the donor locus using a dominant negative selectable marker. The indole acetamide hydrolase (iaaH) gene has been incorporated in the T-DNAs carrying both the Ds elements and the Ac element. Selection against the iaaH gene using napthalene acetamide (NAM), and for the Ds element using kanamycin allows the recovery of transposition events that have lost (by recombination) the donor locus, and thereby enriches for unlinked transposition events. Since we also select against the Ac element, the insertion is immediately stabilized.


4. LABORATORY

A mutagenesis is initiated by crossing plants homozygous for one of the Ds elements to plants containing the Ac transposase. The resulting F1 seed are planted, and the plants are allowed to self-pollinate. The F2 seed are harvested, and plated on media containing kanamycin (selects for the Ds element) and NAM (selects against the iaaH gene). The double resistant F2 seedlings (called transposants) contain a transposed Ds element. The NamR KanR seedlings are transplanted to soil, and allowed to self-fertilize. F3 seed are collected, and grown, and the resulting plants are stained for GUS activity and examined for mutant phenotypes.

You will perform F2 selections and screen F3 seedlings for mutations and patterns of GUS expression during the laboratory. The F3 seedlings you will screen were derived from F2 selections that were made and grown up previously. You will then use a tissue PCR technique to confirm the transposants.

Experiment #1: Selecting F2 Families for NamR KanR transposants

1. You will be given F2 seed aliquoted into approximately 700 seed (15 mg) batches. The seed will be sterilized in 15 ml conical tubes and plated on 100mm square plates.

2. Sterilize the seed by following the protocol below. Work in the hood and use sterile technique.

a. Wash with 2 ml 95% ethanol for 5 min. The seed will settle and the ethanol can be decanted or pipetted off.

b. Add 1 ml of bleach solution to the seed, leave 5 min. Fill tube with sterile water and allow seed to settle. Remove liquid by pipetting or decanting.

c. Wash 2 times with a large volume of sterile water. Decant each wash.

3. Add top agar (at 60° C) to 4 ml.

4. Pour quickly onto plate and swirl to evenly distribute seeds.

5. Let agar solidify.

6. Place at 4°C overnight.

7. Move plates to growth chamber for 4-5 days.

8. Examine plates for seedlings that are resistant to NAM and kanamycin (transposants). You will be shown examples of these phenotypes.

9. Transfer NamR KanR seedlings carefully to small (60mm) Nam/Kan MS plates. Use sterile forceps, and work in the hood. This will allow verification of the double resistant phenotype.

Note: Only about 25% of F2 families give rise to transposants, so not every plate will have them. In addition, transposants arise at frequencies between 0.1% and 2.5%, so the number on any given plate will vary. Examine plates from other groups as well as your own.

Experiment #2: Screening F3 families for mutant phenotypes.

We have approximately 10,000 transposant lines at present. These lines are represented by F3 seed which was collected from individual enhancer and gene trap transposants (each of which was selected as NamR KanR F2 seedling). You will be given F3 seed from both enhancer and gene trap lines.

Plating and Sterilization

1. Sterilize 20 - 30 seeds (these will be aliquoted for you) from each family. The exact number of families will be given to you in the lab. Sterilize in an eppendorf tube using the protocol described below.

a. Wash with ~0.5 ml ethanol for 5 min. Allow seed to settle and pipette or aspirate off ethanol.

b. Add ~0.5 ml bleach solution to seed, leave for 5 min. Do not leave this step longer than 5 minutes, as germination efficiency will decrease. Fill tube with sterile water, allow seed to settle. Remove liquid by aspiration or pipetting.

c. Wash 3 times with sterile water. Aspirate or pipette off each wash.

d. Resuspend seed in 0.7 ml 0.1% agar. Spread on MS non-selective media (60mm plates), distributing evenly.

2. Place plates at 4° C 4 days (until Friday 7/5). Cold treatment encourages even germination.

3. Transfer plates to growth chamber until Sunday July 14.

Phenotypic screen

Examine seedlings for visible phenotypes. We classify these as size variants, morphological defects and pigment defects. Record the number of seedlings that germinate, and the number of mutant and normal seedlings on the plate. Individual F2 plants can be either homozygous or heterozygous for the Ds element, so any insertion induced phenotype could either be present in every F3 plant (in the case of a F2 homozygote) or could segregate 3:1 normal to mutant (if the F2 plant was heterozygous and the mutation is recessive). Segregation ratios substantially less than 1 in 4 may indicate that the mutation arose after transposition and is not associated with the insertion. Mutations in our lines arise at a frequency of about 4%, not all of which are tagged.

Experiment #3. Screening for GUS staining patterns.

Screening for reporter gene expression patterns in the same seedlings can now proceed. Since F2 plants could be either homozygous or heterozygous, insertions may be segregating in the F3. Stain 4 seedlings from each line in order to make sure you sample plants that have the insertion. Record the number of seedlings that stain.

Staining for GUS activity.

You will use two different staining conditions that differ in the concentration of an oxidative catalyst.

1. With fine forceps, carefully transfer seedlings from each plate into a microtiter well containing 0.7 ml of GUS stain.

2. After seedlings are transferred, place dish in dessicator, and draw vacuum for 10 minutes.

3. Wrap dishes in aluminum foil (GUS is light sensitive) and place at 37° C overnight.

-> Save remaining seedlings on plate, you will need them later.

Clearing

Remove stain solution and replace with 70% EtOH. Clearing is speeded up by placing at 37°C. Several changes may be necessary.

Examination

First look at seedlings in microtiter dishes with a dissecting scope. You may want to remove some of the EtOH to get a clearer view. Seedlings showing interesting patterns can be mounted on a microscope slide and looked at under higher magnification.

Experiment #4: Confirmation of Transposants

Ds has a tendency to transpose to closely linked sites (within 5 cM, and often within a few kb). Therefore, insertions into the iaaH gene on the T-DNA are possible. If this happens, the iaaH gene will be inactivated, conferring NAM resistance. We fortuitously select for this event. In practice, we find about 5 percent of our transposants contain an iaaH insertion (and are therefore not really transposants). However, in lines that express GUS, the percentage is higher. We need to test each GUS positive line for the presence of the iaaH gene, which would indicate that the line is not a true transposant. This is easily done using PCR, which can be done on plant tissue using the following protocol (Klimyuk et al., 1993).

You will test each line that expresses GUS. The lines will be divided up so that each group does approximately the same number.

1. Pick a small amount of tissue from each seedling (1/2 cotyledon works well, too much inhibits the reaction). Collect tissue from 3 seedlings from each line (because of Ds segregation) . This will be combined in a single PCR reaction.

2. Collect tissue into a 0.5 ml eppendorf tube containing 40µl of 0.25N NaOH. Boil for 30 seconds in a water bath.

3. Add 40µl 0.25N HCl, and 20µl 0.5M Tris pH8; 0.25% Nonidet P-40. Boil for 2 minutes.

4. Spin, and discard liquid. The plant tissue will be used in a PCR reaction.

5. Set up the following reaction for each sample. Make a master mix containing all reaction components. Make enough for two additional samples to allow for pipetting errors.

5.0 µl 10X PCR buffer

5.0 µl dNTPs (2 mM stock)

2.5 µl Nam1 Primer (10 pmoles/l)

2.5 µl Nam2 Primer (10 pmoles/l)

2.5 µl Nam3 Primer (10 pmoles/l)

2.5 µl Nam4 Primer (10 pmoles/l)

2.5 µl 148D Primer (10 pmoles/l)

2.5 µl 148AR Primer (10 pmoles/l)

0.2 µl Taq Polymerase

24.8 µl Water

The four Nam primers are from two different regions of the iaaH gene, which will allow detection of the gene even if one region has been disrupted by the insertion. The 148 primers are from a chromosomal gene, and provide an internal control for each reaction.

6. Add 50 µl of the mix to each tube (containing the treated tissue). Overlay with 50 µl mineral oil (if using thermocycler without heated lid).




7. Place tubes in thermocycler. The following program will be used

95° C 10 min (1 X)

95° C 30 sec

55° C 30 sec (30 X)

72° C 30 sec

72° C 10 min (1 X)

8. Run 15 µl of each sample on a 2.0% agarose gel.

9. The 148AR-148D primer pair gives about a 175 bp band.

The Nam1 - Nam2 primer pair gives about a 375 bp band.

The Nam3 - Nam4 primer pair gives about a 500 bp band.
