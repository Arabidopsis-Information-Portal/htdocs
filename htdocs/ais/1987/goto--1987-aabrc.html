EFFECT OF GIBBERELLINS ON FLOWER DEVELOPMENT OF THE PIN-FORMED MUTANT OF ARABIDOPSIS THALIANA

N. Goto, M. Starke and A.R. Kranz

Department of Biology, Miyagi College of Education, Aoba-Yama, Sendai 980, Japan

Botanisches Institut, J.W. Goethe-Universitat, Siesmayerstr. 70, D-6000 Frankfurt am Main, FRG

A single gene recessive mutant called the pin formed or 24-a of Arabidopsis thaliana is unable to develop flower organs. This mutant has been induced and selected by H. Kappert (1959), and was firstly demonstrated by A.R. Kranz during the 2nd International Symposium on Arabidopsis Research, 1976, held at Frankfurt/Main.

The pin-formed mutant usually develops secondary shoots lacking any flower organ (Figure 1). Most of the shoots terminate with reduced flower organs, i.e., no petals and/or stamens, and the stems are usually fasciated. The mutant plants are not reproductive and can only be obtained from the heterozygous +/pin genotype whose phenotype is identical with the wildtype.

Since gibberellins applied to the vegetative rosette promote flowering of Arabidopsis, however, there are some reports that gibberellin does not necessarily promote the flowering process although it enhances the bolting of the long day plants (see Bernier et al., 1981). Therefore, investigating the defective function of the pin gene we may expect that the role of gibberellin is normal in the mutant and treatment with GA-substances will not normalize the flower development. Thus, the process of flower induction is separate from the hormonal control of bolting also in Arabidopsis. On the other hand, since short-stemmed GA-defective mutants of Arabidopsis can be normalized to wildtype phenotype by treating with GA (Koornneef, 1980), uncontrolled overproduction of native gibberellins can be alternatively assumed for the pin-mutant.

We have expected the pin-formed mutant is useful to examine the role of gibberellin in the flower development separately from bolting process. Present study aimed to ascertain whether gibberellins are efficient on the development of flower organs using this mutant.

Seeds were obtained from AIS-seed bank (access. no. 2064), selected from mutant F97. The mutant can only be maintained from wildtype phenotypes segregating the mutant type in a Mendelian ratio of 3:1 which shows the parent plants to be heterozygous (+/pin). This sample was harvested from wildtype pheonotypes in F3, thus resembling a mixture of homozygous +/+ and heterozygous +/pin genotypes. The seeds were sown on the soil in pots, and allowed to grow in a green house (20*degrees*C, 16h light/8h dark cycle) after 4 days cold treatment (2*degrees*C, darkness). Gibberellins (GA 4+7 and GA 3) were applied to the leaves (50 micro l/day/plant) from the 4th day after transferring to the greenhouse. Treatment with GA 10E-2 M was interrupted for one week to avoid damage of plants developing rosettes.

Table 1 shows the frequency of the pin-formed plants, which in the absence of any selection is expected to appear in 10% of F 4 generation. With ts-test we concluded that the frequency in appearance of the pin-formed plants was not affected by gibberellins, i.e., gibberellin cannot change the phenotype of the pin-formed mutant.

The stem apex of the pin-formed plant usually becomes sharp like a pin tip. However, some plants develop deformed sterile flower organs at the top of the stem, which are able to be classified into several stages according to their morphological imperfection (Fugure 2). Table 2 shows the effect of gibberellins on coming to perfection of flower morphology which was set on the basis of the indiction in Figure 2. High concentration (10E-2M) of both the gibberellins seemed to decrease the frequency of the plants completely lacking flower organs. This suggests that giberellins may promote the process of flower organization. However, it is necessary to confirm this suggestion by examinations using greater number of the pinformed plants.

Table 3 shows the number of rosette leaves developed by the time of bolting. Gibberellins reduced the number of leaves (i.e., hastened the bolting) in both the normal land pin-formed plants, the effect being greater in GA 10 4+7 than GA 3. This may indicate that gibberellins act to promote the process of shoot elongation of the pin-formed plants in the same manner as of the normal plants.

Conclusion of the preliminary results obtained confirms the independence of the flower development from the gibberellin control of bolting and that native GA is not overproduced in the pin mutant of Arabidopsis. Evidently, defective function of the pin gene is already expressed at the level of flower induction which has to be confirmed by further investigations.
