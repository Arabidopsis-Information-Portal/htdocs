A THEORETICAL MODEL FOR ONE LOCUS APPLICABLE TO POPULATION-GENETIC STUDIES IN ARABIDOPSIS

I. CETL

Department of Genetics Science Faculty J.E. Purkyne University Brno, Czechoslovakia

Arabidopsis thaliana (L.) Heynh. appears to be a reliable experimental object for studying the population structure of predominantly self-pollinating plan species (CETL, and PLCHOVA, 1975; CETL, in press). Conclusions from experimental data can be done only by means of a theoretical model taking into account the three main features of populations of Arabidopsis: (1) the low proportion of random crossing; (2) effective selection pressures against homozygotes; (3) the condition of balanced polymorphism for some loci in equilibrium populations.

One locus, A, with two alleles, A1 and A2, is assumed to exist in a large population of a completely autogamous plant species. The two alleles are present in the population at gene frequencies p and q, constituting the A1A1, A1A2, and A2A2 genotypes in genotypic frequencies P, H, and Q, respectively. A permanent zygotic selection pressure against the two homozygotes is expressed by corresponding adaptive values w1 and w2, w1 being a measure of selection against A1A1, and w2 of selection against A2A2, if w1, w2<1.

More complicated formulae should be constructed for the equilibrium frequency of heterozygotes if complete autogamy will change into a common presence of autogamy and crossing the proportions a and c, respectively. Naturally, a + c = 1.
