SEED SIZE DISTRIBUTIONS AND EFFECTS ON GROWTH IN AUX-1 AND WILD TYPE ARABIDOPSIS

S.J.B. Martindale and E.P. Maher

Department of Genetics, Aberdeen University, Scotland

An examination of seed size distribution in wild type, 2, 4-D-resistant mutant aux-1 (Maher, 1977; Maher and Martindale, 1980; Martindale and Maher, 1980) and F2 seed populations was undertaken, after it was noted that mutant seeds appeared to be bigger than those of the wild type. An attempt was made to quantify this size difference.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Initially, only wild type and aux-1 seed samples were examined.

3163 wild type and 5253 mutant seeds were separated by size using a set of Endecotts test sieves with sieve spacings as follows:

In an attempt to minimise any inter-plant variation seed was collected from 6 individual plants of each genotype and pooled. Wild type and mutant plants were grown to maturity over the same period in the greenhouse and the seed was harvested on the same day.

F2 seed was harvested from 6 F1 plants of the cross (wild type x aux-1).

RESULTS

Immediately after harvesting, samples of pooled seed were taken using a small spoon, hence the difference in seed numbers. The seed was then placed in the top sieve and the tower of sieves was tapped and shaken for 3 min to ensure good separation. Seeds falling into different size classes were counted and the distributions were plotted as histograms.

As an extension of this study seed from the different size classes was sown on 0.75% agar 0.1mM KNO3 and germinated in the growth cabinet. Root lengths were measured at 72 hours and the distribution plotted. Figures 2a, b and c show the results for the wild type seed size classes 250-280 micro m, 212-250 micro m and 200-212 micro m respectively. This series of histograms shows that bigger seeds produce seedlings with longer roots after 72 hours in the growth cabinet confirming the results of Mirza and Maher (1980).

The only appreciable overlap between genotypes is in seed size class 250-280 micro m. Seedlings derived from this class show considerable differences in root length between genotypes on plain agar (compare Figures 2a and 3c). This may result from differences in the distribution of seed sizes within this size class or from an effect of genotype on root growth unrelated to seed size.

Seed from the seed size classes shown was sown on agar/KNO 3 + 2,4-D (0.3 micro m) and root lengths were measured after 72 hours in the growth cabinet.

Again, bigger mutant seed produced seedlings with longer roots. However, both wild type size classes were greatly inhibited.

It is important to note at this stage that dose response data (Maher, 1977) could be confounded if, by chance, small seeds were selected for control plates and large seeds for the test plates, or vice versa. However, these major size differences were not noted until after most of the dose response and growth rate (Mahr and Martindale, 1980) experiments had been completed. Once it was realised that seed size played a major role in seedling growth, seed from seed size class 250-280 micro m only was used to represent both wild type and mutant. This was the size class with sufficient seed of both genotypes for most experiments.

To test the effect of genotype on seed size, and to complete the seed size study, it was decided to examine an F2 population of seed, originating from selfed F1 plants (wild type x mutant). Again, 6 F1 plants were selected, harvested and the seed pooled.

The seed was sieved as for wild type and mutant samples. Table 2 shows the distribution of seed sizes under the same classification as Table 1.

Figure 6 shows the histogram plotted from the data in Table 2.

This result suggests that seed genotype might be responsible for seed size. Seed from F1 plant would fall into one of three classes with regard to resistance to 2, 4-D namely wild type homozygous dominant, F1-type heterozygotes or mutant homozygous recessive (2, 4-D resistant) in an expected ratio 1:2:1. Close examination of Figures 6, 1a and 1b suggests that F1 parent plants produce seed representative of seed size classes of both wild type and mutant.

If auxin sensitivity and seed size were pleitropic a preponderance of aux-1+/aux-1+ genotypes would be expected the smaller size classes with aux-1/aux-1 genotypes in the larger size classes. Table 3 shows clearly that this is not the case suggesting that seed genotype does not govern seed size and that, therefore, the parental (maternal) genotype may be the main factor in determining the differences in seed size distributions shown in Figures 1(a), 1(b), and 6.

CONCLUSION

The major point of the value which emerged from these experiments was the realisation that bigger F2 seed provided a bigger differential between resistant and susceptible seedling root lengths, after 72 hours on 2, 4-D containing plates, than did smaller F 2 seed samples. This finding could be of particular value in the identification of segregating resistant seedlings where a less resistant mutant is concerned.

Workers using Arabidopsis thaliana as a research tool should be aware that seed size variation could be a possible source of experimental error. It is suggested that seed size standardisation would be a useful procedure in Arabidopsis research.
