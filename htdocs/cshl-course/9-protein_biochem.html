1. INTRODUCTION

Antibodies are valuable tools to study the properties, localization and abundance of proteins (Harlow and Lane, 1988). The immunoblot (western blot) is one of the most frequently used immunotechniques for the following reasons: - Proteins are separated by electrophoresis prior to immunodetection, eliminating many problems related to non-specific binding of the antibody. - The detection is not limited to soluble proteins because detergent and reducing agent are present in the denaturing gel sample buffer. - Antigens can be quantified from a very small amount of tissue (as little as 10 mg or less). - The same membrane can be stripped and reprobed with several antibodies.

Screening for Regulatory Mutants is a very useful approach to study the regulation of a gene. The biochemical study of a gene's regulation often provides clues on the possible ways of isolating regulatory mutants. One strategy that has been applied to study gene regulation utilizes the promoter-reporter transgenic plant as a starting material. This strategy's advantage is that the expression of the reporter gene reflects the regulation of the gene in study, and that the activity of the reporter gene can be easily evaluated. One reporter gene commonly used for this type of screening is ß- glucorunadase (GUS) (Bowling et al., 1994; Cao et al., 1994). The detection of GUS activity can be accomplished by histochemical staining with X-Gluc or by enzyme assays using MUG. The advantages of using GUS as the reporter gene in a mutant screen include: 1) transgenic plants are usually already constructed in order to study the regulation of genes of interest; and 2) X- Gluc staining and MUG assay require very little tissue so the screen can be done with a single small leaf. One disadvantage of the GUS system is that the screening is very labor intensive. An alternative reporter gene is the hph gene which confers resistance to hygromycin B (Susek et al., 1993). Thus, selection on hygromycin B is possible. However, transgenic plants that have hph as the reporter gene are uncommon, and therefore it is almost certain that the transgenic plants will have to be constructed.

The tryptophan biosynthetic pathway leads to the biosynthesis of many secondary metabolites, in addition to tryptophan (Radwanski and Last, 1995). For example, the major phytoalexin from Arabidopsis, camalexin, is derived from the tryptophan biosynthetic pathway (Tsuji et al., 1993; Glazebrook and Ausubel, 1994). The diverse functions of the products derived from this pathway probably contribute to the fact that many conditions such as amino acid starvation and pathogen infection have been found to induce the tryptophan pathway enzymes at both the mRNA and protein levels.

In this lab, we will work with two polyclonal antibodies, anti-TSA and -TSB (Zhao and Last, 1995), against the Arabidopsis tryptophan synthase alpha (TSA) and ß (TSB) subunits, respectively. We will use these antibodies to characterize proteins from Arabidopsis Col-0 and two Arabidopsis mutants, trp2-8 and trp3-1, defective in TSB and TSA respectively (Last and Fink, 1988; Barczak et al., 1995; Radwanski et al., 1995). We will also analyze the induction of TSA and TSB in plants treated with a pathogen, glyphosate, and lysine + threonine. In addition, we are going to screen for constitutive and non-responsive mutants using PAT-GUS transgenic plants (aka AR208).

3. EXPERIMENTAL PROTOCOL

Part A.1: Screening for Regulatory Mutants of the Tryptophan Biosynthetic Pathway

1. Sterilize M2 seeds from mutagenized PAT-GUS transgenic plants (AR208) and resuspend in 0.1% agar. Use a pasteur pipette to plate out 50 seeds on a PN plate in a sterile hood. Use a grid (fifty 1cm X 1cm squares) to guide the plating. Grow the plants for 2 weeks under constant light (about 90 µE). Always plate out unmutagenized AR208 as a control. (this step has already been done).

2. To screen for constitutive mutants, excise 1 cotyledon from each plant using sterile tissue culture scissors. Stain the cotyledons for GUS in 150 µl X-Gluc solution in a 96 well plate. Plants with a higher GUS staining when compared to the AR208 control are putative constitutive mutants. Transfer these putative mutants onto soil.

3. To screen for nonresponsive mutants, treat the plates with 3 ml 40 mM K+T. Two days after the K+T treatment, excise one cotyledon from each plant and stain for GUS. The GUS transgene in parental AR208 (control) should be induced by K+T. M2 plants that show a lower GUS staining are picked as the non-responsive putative mutants. Transfer these putative mutants onto soil.

** Constitutive mutants and nonresponsive mutants can be screened in a sequential order using the same plate. In this case, screen for constitutives first and then do the K+T treatment and the nonresponsive mutant screen.

4. Grow the putative mutants and harvest the M3 seeds.

5. Rescreen the M3 seed of the putative mutants using steps #1-3. After GUS staining, perform Western blots on any interesting lines to check the levels of endogenous protein expression. In this way, you can eliminate any mutations which only affect the expression/activity of GUS.

Part A.2: Chemical treatment of Arabidopsis plants

Arabidopsis seeds were surface sterilized (please refer to enhancer-trap lab protocol for sterilizing seeds) and fifty seeds were plated out on each PN(S) plate. The plates were grown under 60-120 µE light for 14 days. Three ml of a chemical solution (glyphosate or lysine + threonine) were added to each plate and the solution was evenly spread out by vigorously shaking the plates horizontally. Glyphosate and lysine + threonine solutions were used at 15 mM and 40 mM respectively. The final concentration will be 10 times lower then the above concentration due to the 30 ml agar volume in each plate. The treated plants were put back to the growth chamber. Plant tissue for immunoblot analysis can be harvested and frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored in a -80 °C freezer.

Part B: Protein Sample Preparation from Arabidopsis Leaves

Each student will prepare 6 samples; each group of two students will need nine different samples for their protein gel (see Part D, step 7 for list of samples).

1. Harvest 0.1 g of leaf tissue, add 300 µl of lysis buffer and homogenize with a pestle for Eppendorf tubes. Keep tubes on ice while you finish the homogenization steps for the other samples. However, it is best to proceed to the next step upon accumulating a group of 4 or 6 protein samples.

2. Spin the tubes for 5 min in a microfuge (12, 000g) at 4 °C.

3. Add 80 µl of 2X SDS sample buffer to a new tube for each sample.

4. Transfer 80 µl of the supernatent to the tubes with the 2X SDS sample buffer (henceforth known as sample A). Add 10 µl of the supernatent from the spun tubes to another tube (sample B).

5. Cap each sample A tube with a safety lock and boil for five minutes. After boiling, these can sit at room temperature. These will be used for the protein gel.

6. Keep sample B tubes on ice. These will used later to determine the protein concentration.

7. Continue steps #1-6 until done extracting protein from the plant tissue samples.

Part C: Bradford Protein Concentration Assay

1. Dilute BioRad protein assay stock solution with double distilled water (1 part stock sol.: 9 parts water).

2. Add 1 ml of the diluted assay solution to an eppendorf tube. You will need one tube for each sample and two tubes to use as a blank.

3. Add 5 µl of sample B to the assay solution.

4. Measure A595 using a disposable cuvette.

5. Determine the protein concentration for each sample. Add 1X SDS sample buffer, if necessary, to each sample A tube in order to equilibrate the concentrations to one another.

Part E: Protein Transfer

1. After running the gel, put the gel in transfer buffer for about 20 min with gentle shaking. Soak the nitrocellulose membrane in the transfer buffer for about 10 min.
-> Use S&S NC 0.2 µm pore; Note: this is not the same as S&S BAS NC used for RNA and DNA gel blots.

2. Assemble the transfer sandwich in a glass dish containing transfer buffer with the order of: black side of plastic sandwich down in tray then wet fiber pad, wet Whatman 3MM filter paper, gel, wet membrane, wet filter paper, wet fiber pad then clear side of plastic sandwich. All components are pre-wet in transfer buffer.

3. Put the sandwich into the blotting chamber with the membrane on the anode (+) side; in other words, place the black side of the sandwich facing the black side of the sandwich holder. Put cold transfer buffer and the ice cooler into the chamber.

4. Blotting conditions:
1 h blot: 100V with prechilled buffer, ice cooler, and constant stirring.
3-5 h blot: 40-50V.
Overnight blot: 30V
-> We are going to do the overnight transfer.

Day 2

Part F: Immunoblot

1. Stain the membrane in Ponceau S solution for 10 min and rinse with water.
-> Take a picture of the stained membrane.

2. Block the membrane in Blotto for >1 h at room temperature.
-> 4 h to overnight can be used to reduce the background.

3. Incubate the membrane with 1000X diluted anti-TSA and anti-TSB (together in Blotto) with gentle shaking for 2 hours at room temperature.

4. Wash the membrane with TBS or Blotto for three times with gentle shaking for 5-10 minutes each time.

5. Put the membrane into fresh Blotto containing the secondary antibody (5000X dilution of an alkaline phosphatase conjugated goat anti-rabbit antibody) and incubate with gentle shaking for at least 30 minutes.

6. Wash with 1X TBS for three times with gentle shaking for 5-10 min.

7. Develop color in 20 ml alkaline phosphatase buffer with 50 µl of each substrate, p-nitro blue tetrazolium chloride (NBT) and 5-bromo-4-chloro-3- indolylphosphate p-toluidine salt (BCIP). Let the color develop for 10 min or until the band of interest shows up. Stop the reaction by rinsing the membrane with water.

8. Analyze the results and identify the two mutants (determine whether they are trp2-8 or trp3-1).

4. ADDITIONAL PROTOCOLS


Part A: Using Radiolabeled Secondary Reagents

1. After the first antibody binding and washing step (step 33), transfer the nitrocellulose membrane to a square petri-dish. Add 30 ml of fresh Blotto and 1 mCi of 125I protein A.
-> 125I protein A is supplied by New England Nuclear (NEN), NEX-146L, 100 mCi.

2. Incubate the membrane for 1-2 hours at room temperature with gentle shaking.

3. Remove the 125I protein A solution into a radioactive liquid waste container. Add 15 ml TBS to the dish and shake for 10 min. Remove the TBS into a radioactive waste container.
-> The 125I protein A solution can be reused several times.

5. Transfer the membrane to a tray containing 250 ml of TBS and wash for 10 minutes. Discard the TBS into the sink and flush with water.

6. Wash 2-3 times in TBS, 10 minutes each.

7. Remove the filter and seal it in a heat seal-bag.

8. Expose the membrane to a phosphoimager film at room temperature or to an X-ray film at -70°C with an intensifying screen.

Part B: Stripping and Reprobing Membranes with Antibodies

1. Submerge the membrane in stripping buffer and incubate at 50°C for 30 min.

2. Wash the membrane in TBS at room temperature twice for 10 minutes each.

3. Block the membrane in blocking solution (Blotto) for at least 1 hour.

4. Perform regular immunoblot.
