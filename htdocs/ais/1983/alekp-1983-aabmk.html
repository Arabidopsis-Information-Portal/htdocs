ARABIDOPSIS THALIANA AS TEST OBJECT FOR STUDYING ANTIMUTAGENESIS

U.K. Alekperov, D.D. Akhundova, G.G. Mirza-Zadeh and S.Sh. Agamamedova

N.K. Koltzov-Institute of Developmental Biology, Acad. Sci. USSR, Vavilov St. 26, Moscow, USSR

The present-day ecological situation is characterized by the presence of mutagens and carcinogens in the environment many of which have become permanent or long-duration components of the biosphere. In this context, the problem of anti-mutagenesis is gaining a particular importance.

Until recently antimutagenesis of higher plants was studied on a chromosome level (Welsh onion, aegilops, beans, hawk's beard, wheat) (Alekperov, 1982)

In this paper the first results are reported on using Arabidopsis thaliana as a test object to analyse the effect of antimutagens on induced mutations in higher plants. The experiments included irradiating both control and experimental seeds of A. thaliana race Enkheim with gamma rays at a dose of 500, 300, 100 and 50 Gray followed by treating experimental seeds with aqueous enotannin solution at a concentration of 1 x 10E-3 micro g/ml for 18 hours. The seeds were then placed in test tubes containing agarized mineral medium and planted at permanent illumination and a temperature of 24 +/- 1*degree*C. Unripe pods of A. thaliana were analysed using embryo test by Muller for the presence of chlorophyll mutations (Muller, 1961).

It is seen from Table 1 that the level of induced mutations correlates well with irradiation dose for both control and experiment species. The share of mutated plants varies between 3.9 +/- 2.24% for 50 Gray and 27.4 +/- 4.33 for 500 Gray.

The use of enotannin resulted in a considerable reduction of mutation plants at any irradiation dose.

It appears that due to the well known advantages of A. thaliana which made this plant, among some others, most adequate for investigating gene mutations, it can be used in another direction of modern genetics, namely in antimutagenesis.
