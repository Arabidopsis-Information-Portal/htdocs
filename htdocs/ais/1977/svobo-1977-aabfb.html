MEASURING ADAPTIVE VALUES IN ARABIDOPSIS

Marie Svobodova and I. Cetl

Department of Genetics, J.E. Purkyne University Brno, Czechoslovakia

The adaptive value of a genotype represents a relative measure of Darwinian fitness expressing the mean number of descendants left by the representatives of this genotype as compared with another (reference) genotype. At least two components take part in constituting fitness: the ability to survive from zygote to adult, and the fertility of the latter. The two compoennts can be also given in relative units as relative viability and relative fertility, respectively. In spite of this, relative viablilities were often substituted for adaptive values (cf. Allard, Jain and Workman, 1968). In Arabidopsis thaliana, Cetl and Plchova (1975) attempted to express the adaptive value, w, as a product of relative viability, v, and relative fertility, f:w = vf.

Seven artificial populations each of them carrying one recessive allele for a certain visible mutation, m, were established. In the generation t = O, seeds were harvested from m+m heterozygotes so that po = qo = .5 and Ho = 1 (cf. Cetl, 1976). In the next generation, t=1, relative viabilities, v1 and v2, and relative fertilities, f1 and f2, were measured in four replications with 200 seeds sown in each by means of the survival rates and the seed numbers in the two homozygotes, m+m+ and mm, respectively, and expressed in relative units as compared with the heterozygote, m+m. Because of the unlimited apical growth of the inflorescences only seed ripening during 10 days after the harvest of the first pod of the plant were counted.

It is evident that there is no clear relation between the viability and fertility in particular populations. Thus relative viability by itself cannot be used in predicting the further development of the population.

The adaptive values of the dominant homozygote were generally twice compared with those of the recessive homozygote. In five populations (with mutant alleles L, lc, lc(S), ln, and chm3) transitional polymorphism might be expected on the basis of theoretical considerations for one locus (Cetl, 1976). It means that heterozygote advantage seems to be wide-spread in Arabidopsis not only in the genetic systems with lethal chlorophyll mutations (Cetl and Plchova, 1975) but also in those with visible mutations.

It seems that the way described for measuring adaptive values in Arabidopsis is applicable also for other plant species.
