1. Introduction

Like other organisms, plants are subject to attack by a wide variety of microorganisms, including viruses, bacteria, and fungi. Plants differ from animals in two ways which have profound effects on the strategies plants use to defend themselves from pathogen attack. First, plants do not have an immune system, so, since there are no cells specialized for defense of the organism, each cell must have the capacity to respond to a pathogen attack. Second, plants can suffer loss of significant fractions of the plant body without dire consequences for the survival of the organism. This allows "scorched earth" strategies, in which infected cells are killed along with the pathogen, to be effective as plant defense responses.

Plant defense reponses include the hypersensitive response, synthesis of phytoalexins (small antimicrobial compounds), lignification of plant cell walls, synthesis of lytic enxymes such as chitinases and glucanases, an oxidative burst (release of active oxygen species), and expression of a plethora of pathogenesis related proteins and transcripts of unknown function (4, 10). Elucidation of the regulatory mechanisms controlling expression of these defense responses, and the extent to which partiuclar responses are effective in limiting growth of various pathogens is important for the design of strategies to improve disease resistance in crops. Development of disease-resistant lines allows efficient crop production with reduced reliance on environmentally undesirable toxic agrichemicals.

In this laboratory excercise, you will observe two plant defense responses, the hypersenstive response (HR) and phytoalexin synthesis. The HR occurs in plant pathogen interactions in which the plant host specifically recognizes the pathogen and responds by rapidly expressing a battery of defense responses. Recognition requires the presence of an avirulence gene in the pathogen and a corresponding resistance gene in the host. In most cases, a particular plant resistance gene confers resistance only against pathogens carrying a particular avirulence gene, and vice versa. For this reason, this type of resistance has been called "gene-for-gene". A simple molecular model that explains these phenomena is that the plant resistance genes encode receptors that bind the products of the pathogen avirulence genes, and trigger a signal transduction cascade leading to expression of defense responses (9). In nearly all gene-for-gene resistance responses, the defense responses induced in response to pathogen recognition include the HR, a rapid programmed cell death at the infection site. The HR is thought to limit pathogen growth by depriving the pathogen of water and nutrients. The correlation between gene-for-gene resistance and the HR suggests that the HR is an important component of resistance to avirulent pathogens. In the field, plants normally encounter low titers of pathogens, so only isolated plant cells undergo the HR and the plant shows few, if any, symptoms resulting from its interaction with a pathogen. In the laboratory, plants can be infected with extremely high pathogen titers, so that the HR can be detected macroscopically as dry necrotic patches of tissue.

In experiment 1, you will infect various wild-type Arabidopsis plants, and plants with one of the resistance gene mutations rps2 (14) or rpm1 (1) with a virulent P. syringae strain and with isogenic strains carrying one of the avirulence genes avrRpt2 (5, 13) or avrRpm1 (3). You will observe disease symptoms caused by the virulent strain, the HR and resistance that result from avirulence gene-resistance gene interactions, and the gene-for-gene specificity of the resistance.

Experiments 2 and 3 address phytoalexin synthesis and its antimicrobial activity. Phytoalexins are low molecular weight antimicrobial compounds produced by plants in response to pathogen attack (11) . Different plant families produce different classes of phytoalexins. For example, legumes produce isoflavonoids, potatoes produce sesquiterpenoids, and brassicas produce indolics. Several lines of evidence suggest that phytoalexins play an important role in plant defense. Phytoalexins inhibit growth of bacterial and fungal phytopathogens in vitro (2). In many cases, phytoalexins accumulate more rapidly in response to infection by avirulent pathogens than in response to virulent ones (2, 10) . Constitutive expression of a gene encoding stilbene synthase, a phytoalexin biosynthetic enzyme from grape, in transgenic tobacco conferred increased resistance against a tobacco fungal pathogen (8) .

Many plants produce multiple, structurally related phytoalexins, but only one phytoalexin (3-thiazol-2-ylindole, or camalexin) has been detected in significant quantities in Arabidopsis (12). Camalexin inhibits the growth of the fungal pathogen, Cladosporium cladosporoides, and the bacterial pathogen, Pseudomonas syringae, in vitro (12). Camalexin is synthesiszed in response to P. syringae infection, but there is no clear correlation between pathogen avirulence and camalexin accumulation (6). Mutations in at least five Arabidopsis genes (pad genes) cause defects in camalexin synthesis in response to P. syringae infection. None of the pad mutants are compromised for resistance to P. syringae strains carrying avirulence genes, demonstrating that camalexin does not play a crucial role in conferring gene-for-gene type resistance to these pathogens (6) . However, mutations in four of the five pad genes cause increased susceptibility to virulent P. syringae strains, suggesting that camalexin may contribute to limiting the growth of these pathogens (6, 7).

In experiment 2 of the laboratory excercise, you will isolate camalexin from Arabidopsis leaves using extraction with organic solvents and thin layer chromatography. You will observe that camalexin is synthesized in response to infection. In experiment 3, you will use a bioassay to show that camalexin inhibits fungal growth in vitro.

2. Experiment 1: Inoculation of plants with bacterial pathogens.

In this experiment, wild-type plants will be infected with a virulent bacterial pathogen, and with isogenic strains carrying one of the avirulence genes avrRpt2 or avrRpm1. Symptoms caused by virulent bacteria, resistance conferred by recognition of an avirulence gene, and the hypersensitive response associated with resistance will be observed. In addition, rps2 mutants (with loss-of-function mutations in the RPS2 resistance gene, which is required for resistance to bacteria carrying avrRpt2), and rpm1 mutants (with loss-of-function mutations in the RPM1, resistance gene, which is required for resistance to bacteria carrying avrRpm1) will be infected with the virulent and avirulent strains. The gene-for-gene specificity of the hypersensitive response and resistance will be observed.

PROCEDURE

1. The night before the experiment, inoculate 5 ml cultures of PsmES4326, PsmES4326(avrRpt2), and PsmES4326(avrRpm1). Use King's B medium, and add tetracycline to 5 µg/ml for the PsmES4326(avrRpt2) and PsmES4326(avrRpm1) cultures. Shake at 28° overnight.

2. Pellet 1 ml of each culture in a microcentrifuge for 1 minute. Remove the supernatant, resuspend the cells in 1 ml of 10mM MgSO4, and pellet again. Resuspend cells in 1 ml of 10mM MgSO4.

3. Dilute the cells 1:10 into 10mM MgSO4, and measure OD600. For each strain, prepare 10 ml of an OD600=0.1 suspension, and 10 ml of an OD600=0.002 suspension.

4. Inoculate several mature leaves of each plant genotype (Wild-type Columbia, rps2 mutant, and rpm1 mutant) with each bacterial suspension, as well as sterile 10 mM MgSO4 solution. Inoculate by filling a 1 ml syringe (without a needle) with the bacterial suspension, pressing it against the abaxial surface (underside) of the leaf, and gently depressing the plunger. Label the infected leaves by attaching tape to toothpicks, writing the inoculum used on the tape, and inserting the toothpicks into the soil on the clockwise side of the infected leaves.

Expected results: Hypersensitive responses should be visible by 16 hours after inoculation. Leaves infected with OD600=0.1 concentrations of virulent strains will show a water-soaked collapse starting at 24 hours after inoculation. Virulent strains infected with OD600= 0.002 concentrations will cause water-soaking and chlorosis visible after 2-3 days.

Medium

King's B

10 g Difco proteose peptone #3

1.5 g K2HPO4, anhydrous

15 g glycerol

H2O to 1 liter

Adjust pH to 7.0 with HCl

After autoclaving, add sterile MgSO4 to 5mM

3. Experiment 2: Detection of camalexin by1 fluorescence.

The phytoalexin produced by Arabidopsis, camalexin, is easily detected because it emits a bright blue fluorescence under illumination with long-wave ultraviolet light. In this experiment, camalexin will be extracted from infected leaf tissue, and separated from other components of the extract by thin-layer chromatography (TLC). Since steps 1-3 require more time than is available for this laboratory excercise, extracts will be provided, and the experiment will begin at step 4.

PROCEDURE

1. Two days before the experiment, inoculate three wild-type Arabidopsis leaves with PsmES4326 at a concentration of OD600=0.02.

2. Excise the infected leaves, and three uninfected leaves. Place each leaf into a 1.5 ml eppendorf tube, add 350 µl of 80% methanol (v/v), and heat at 65° for 20 minutes.

3. Remove the leaves, and place the methanol extracts under reduced pressure until the volume is reduced to approximately 100 µl.

4. Extract each sample with 75 µl of choroform, and remove the chloroform phases (the green lower phases) to clean tubes. Allow the choroform to evaporate under reduced pressure (in a glass desiccator, or a vacuum oven with the heat off, attached to house vacuum). This should take about one hour.

5. While the chloroform is evaporating, add 200 ml ethyl acetate and 30 ml hexane to a thin layer chromatography tank in a fume hood. Place the lid on the tank. It is important to have the liquid and gas phases in the tank equilibrated.

6. Dissolve the samples (now a green residue) in 5 µl of choroform. Spot all 5 µl onto a TLC plate, approximately 2 cm from the bottom of the plate, leaving 2 cm between each sample. Samples may be labeled using a #2 pencil (gently, so as not to scrape the silica off the plate). Place the labels below the samples, so that the samples do not pass over them as they develop.

7. Place the plate into the TLC tank, minimizing the time that the lid is off the tank. Allow the chromatogram to develop until the solvent front is 2-4 cm from the top of the plate. This usually takes 45-60 minutes, depending on the temperature in the fume hood.

8. Remove the plate from the tank, and allow it to dry in the fume hood for 10 minutes. Examine the plate in a darkened room using a hand-held long-wave ultraviolet lamp. Camalexin should be visible as a bright blue spot, present only in samples prepared from infected leaves. Mark the center of the camalexin spots lightly with a pencil.

4. Experiment 3: Bioassay for compounds with antimicrobial activity

Compounds with anti-microbial activity can be detected on TLC plates using a bioassay. In this experiment, TLC plates are sprayed with a suspension of fungal spores and hypae in a nutrient solution. The fungus grows over the surface of the plate, except in areas where inhibitory compounds are present. Since the particular fungus used has dark-colored hypae, inhibitory spots are visible as white areas in a dark background.

PROCEDURE

1. Pipet 10 ml of 2X potato-dextrose medium onto the surface of a petri dish that is completely covered by a 2-week-old culture of Cladosporium cladosporoides grown at room temperature on 1X potato-dextrose agar. Gently scrape the surface of the fungal mat with a pipet to suspend spores and hypae in the solution. Filter the suspension through cheesecloth into a chromatography sprayer. Repeat with a second 10 ml aliquot of 2X potato-dextrose medium.

2. Place the TLC plate from Experiment 2 in a vertical position. Spray the plate with the fungal suspension until the plate looks uniformly wet. Do not spray the plate so heavily that liquid runs down it.

3. Line the bottom of a 20 x 20 cm petri dish with a moistened paper towel. Place the sprayed plate on it, cover with the lid, and put in a dark place at room temperature. Zones of inhibition around the camalexin spots should be visible in 1-3 days.
