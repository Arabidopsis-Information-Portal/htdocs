MORE 2,4-D RESISTANT MUTANTS

J. Mirza and E.P. Maher

Department of Genetics, Aberdeen University, Scotland

In previous communications we have described a mutant P83, which is resistant to 2, 4-D and has roots displaying an altered geotropic response (Maher, 1977; Maher and Martindale, 1978, 1980). In this paper we describe two other mutant lines, one of which has lower levels of resistance than P83 but displays normal geotropism, and one in which very high levels of resistance are associated with dwarf plant form and ageotropic roots.

Table 1 gives the levels of resistance shown by these two mutants and by P83 in separate dose-response experiments with wild type included in each case. These experiments were carried out as described in Maher and Martindale (1980).

The low resistance mutant is indistinguishable from wild type on criteria other than resistance. Unlike P83 it has apparently normal geotropic response and although the results of some experiments have shown a slightly increased rate of root growth over wild type, the difference is not consistent. The relative ages of the seed stocks used may be an important factor here. However, regardless of growth rate dose-response cures have given highly consistent differences between P391 and wild type.

When studying low levels of resistance it is important to avoid the confounding effects on root length of other factors. One such factor which may have found to be very variable in seed stocks is seed size. To assess this variability and its effects on root length we have fractionated seed using sieves of different mesh size and measured the length of roots produced by the different seed classes when germinated on plain agar.

The following histograms illustrate some features of the genetics of P391. Figure 1 shows the root length of wild type, P391, and F1 and F2 generations on agar plus 0.27 microM 2, 4-D. The results suggest that the P391 phenotype is determined by a single fully recessive allele. Figure 2 shows the result of the complementation test between P391 and P83 and the resulting F1 and F2 generations, on agar plus 0.73 microM 2, 4-D. The higher 2, 4-D concentration allows greater differentiation between the two mutants. The F1 shows an intermediate level of resistance although both mutations are separately almost fully recessive which suggests that P391 and P83 are allelic. In the F2 the distribution of root lengths conforms with the expected 1 : 2 : 1 ratio of P391: F1 : P83 phenotypes. If P391 and P83 are allelic it will be intriguing to determine if the levels of georesponse in F2 seedlings are correlated with levels of 2, 4-D resistance.

In the high resistance line P445, two types of seedling are segregating. On perlite without 2, 4-D young seedlings develop either the normal sized rosette or a very small, dark green rosette with roots which are ageotrop and often coiled up on the surface of the perlite. Many of these latter seedlings die but survivors produce very short inflorescences which carry fertile flowers and set suficient seed for genetic studies. It is the dwarf phenotypes which show resistance to 2, 4-D although only a small proportion of them survive with or without 2, 4-D. There is no evidence or any correction of the dwarf genotype by the auxin. There is a 3 : 1 ratio of small : large rosettes on perlite (see Tabel 3) and the same ratio is found for resistance: susceptible seedlings on agar plus 2, 4-D. It seems likely that resistance and dwarf phenotypes are pleiotropic effects of a single dominant mutation. To date we have been unable to isolate a pure breeding "high resistance" line from P445, which suggests that homozygotes do not survive to produce inflourescences.

Because of the segregation in P445 the resistance level in Table 1 has been estimated for the "resistant" seedlings in dose response curves. These are easily distinguished from "susceptibles" because the latter are completely inhibited at concentrations which do not affect the resistant types.

We are at present trying to improve the survival of P445 resistant seedlings and to determine the relationships between the genetics and physiology of the three classes of resistant mutants so far available to us.
