CONDITIONS FOR FLOWER INDUCTION IN DARK-GROWN ARBIDOPSIS PLANTS

Nobuharu Goto

Department of Biology, Miyagi College of Education, Aoba-Yama, Sendai 980, Japan

Redei and Acedo (1976) have reported that Arabidopsis plants flowered and even set fruits under the conditions of complete darkness on liquid media. On the contrary, Kranz (1979) has failed to yield flowers on the liquid media under complete darkness. The aim of present study was to investigate the conditions for flower induction under darkness.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Germination: The line Estland, En2n and Sen of Arabidopsis thaliana were used. The line En2n was kindly supplied by Dr. Kranz and line Sen has been bred in our laboratory. The seed were sterilized with filtrate of calcium hypochlorite (5%) for 10 min, rinsed 3 times with sterilized water and sowed on a 15 ml Nitsch's nutrient medium with 3% sucrose (pH 6.0) (Nitsch and Nitsch, 1967) solidified with 0.8% agar in a petri dish 24 hr. Then, the planted seeds were irradiated by white light (about 5000 lux; cool-white fluorescent lamps) for desired periods for germination, and transferred to darkness (25*degrees*C).

Incubation: After 3 days, 2 seedlings were planted on a 5 ml liquid medium in a test tube (18 x 180 mm) in which glass beads were submerged in order to keep the plants not to be under the medium. The medium was the same as that used in germination (minus agar). The surface of the meidum comes to about 1 cm over the uppermost glass beads. The diameters of the beads wre 3, 5 and 7 mm. For the rotatory incubation, 2 seedlings ere planted on a 5 ml liquid meidum in a test tube (16 x 160 mm), and the tubes were settled almost horizontally on a rotator which was turned vertically 7 round per hr. The planting procedures wee carried out udner dim green lgiht. After 5-6 weeks incubation in the dark at 25*degrees*C, the umber of the platns forming flower primordia was observed with a magnifier.

RESULTS

The frequency of flower-induced plants was affected by the size of glass beads (Table 1).

Table 1: Frequency of flower-induced plants grown on the medium including various sizes of glass beads and on a rotator in Arabidopsis thaliana (line Estland). The seeds were irradiated by white light (about 5000 lux) for 5 hr for germination and allowed to grow in the dark for 6 weeks.

The plants grown on the large beads (7 mm in diameter) formed flowers fewer than those on small beads (3 and 5 mm in diameter). The plants grown on the rotator took markedly vigorous roots, and the inner surface of the test tube was covered by numerous roots. Table 2 shows the effect of the period of light irradiation for germination on the flower induction.

The longer the light irradiation was, the greater the frequency of flowering plants was in all the lines. This result suggests tht the factor produced by irradiation at the germination period may be retained several weeks and acted to the flower induction. Light irradiated intermittently for short periods (3 hr light-24 hr dark-1 hr) on the flower induction (except En2n).
