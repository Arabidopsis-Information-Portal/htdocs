A PROVISIONAL MAP OF CHROMOSOME 4 OF ARABIDOPSIS

M. Koornneef, J.H. De Bruine and Philippine Goettsch

Department of Genetics, Agricultural University, Wageningen, The Netherlands

INTRODUCTION

Information on linkage in Arabidopsis is still scanty. Parts of chromosomes on which a number of genes have been arranged linearly are known of chromosome 1 (Feenstra, 1978), chromosome 2 (Redei and Hirono, 1964), and linkage group 4 (Hirono and Redei, 1965). To map the physiological mutants isolated at our laboratory, and to extend the Arabidopsis gene map in general, a number of genes were located on the different chromosomes by trisomic analysis (Koornneef and Van der Veen, 1978). This led to a proper assigning of linkage groups to chromosomes (Koornneef and Den Besten, 1979).

The location of markers on chromosomes was followed by classical linkage analyses. In this report preliminary results are given of linkage studies that involve 8 markers of chromosome 4. This chromosome was shown to carry the linkage group 6 marker vc-2 (which we renamed cer-2) (Koornneef and Den besten, 1979). The information thus far available for this linkage group was limited to reports of linkage relative to vc-2. Redei (1967) mentioned close linkage of cc-2 with im. Robbelen (1972) linkage of vc-2 with a chlorophyll marker repesented by the mutants V89, V300, V312 and V318. A recombinant fraction of 0.185 +/- 0.033 of vc-2 and the lethal chlorophyll marker ch 42 was reported by Fischerova (1975). Linkage of vc-2 with lu as described by the latter author could not be confirmed by Koornneef and Den Besten (1979).

MATERIAL AND METHODS

Marker genes

All markers used for the experiments were recessive mutants induced at Wageningen by EMS in the pure line Landsberg "erecta", except the vc-2 (cer-2) line used in the cross ga-1/cer-2 which was received from Dr. Relichova at Brno. The cer-2 line used in other crosses with these markers was the G8 line described by Dellaert et al., 1979, which was found to be allelic to vc-2 (cer-2). Calculation of recombination fractions and map distances

The majority of the recombination percentages were estimated from F2 data by the Product Ratio Method using the tables of Stevens (1939).

When not all phenotypes could be distinguished recombination fractions were etimated by the method of maximum likelihood. This was the case in crosses of ag/cp with ga-1, where the compact growth habit of the cp mutants could not be distinguished in ga-1 background.

From the estimates of the recombination fractions, map distances were estimated according to the method of Jensen and Jorgensen (1975). This method was applied with recombination percentages corrected for double crossovers by the mapping function of Kosambi (see also Jensen and Jorgensen, 1975). When linkage between two markers was not significant, then the estimates of recombination fractions between them was not used for the ultimate calculation of map distances. The order of the genes was determined from the estimated recombination percentages and from the presence or absence of joint recombination in multiple heterozygotes.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

The estimates of the recombination percentages are presented in Table 2. The figures in Figure 1 give the map distances (cM) after correction by the KOSAMBI mapping function. The ultimate map distances estimated by the method of Jensen and Jorgensen are shown in Figure 2. The analyses show that the order of the genes as proposed in Figure 1 seems well established except perhaps for cer-2. The recombination percentages with its closely linked markers have large standard deviations since they are mainly based on repulsion phase F2 analysis. The location of cer-2 between ag and ga-5 is rather arbitrary since the results obtained do not exclude a position between f ca and ag. The total map length that could be calculated assuming the latter position of cer-2 had a stnadard deviation only slightly larger than the length of 70.4 +/- 3.0 cM calculated for the recombination percentages and gene order as shown in Figure 1. By its total length the provisional map of chromosome 4 is the largest linkage map published for Arabidopsis up to now. However, maps of chromosome 1 and 5 are larger and exceed a length of 100 cM (Koornneef et al., unpublished). Thus the genetical length of the individual Arabidopsis chromosomes is now of the same order as that of e.g. tomato and maize (Rick, 1974; Neuffer and Coe, 1974). Construction of gene maps of all other Arabidopsis chromosomes is in progress.
