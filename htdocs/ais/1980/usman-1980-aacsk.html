ON THE MECHANISM OF THE PHENOMENON OF MAXIMUM MUTATION IN ARABIDOPSIS THALIANA FOLLOWING TREATMENT OF SEEDS WITH DIFFERENT CONCENTRATIONS OF ETHYLENEIMINE

P.D. Usmanov, V.G. Tulakin, and I.G. Mednik

Department of General Genetics of Cotton, Academy of Sciences of the Tajik SSR, Dushanbe, USSR

During the analysis of efficiency of various mutagens in several cases the phenomenon of "maximum mutations" is observed. Many hypotheses explaining this phenomenon are suggested, the most significant of them can be found in monographs of Soyfer and Dubinin (Soyfer, 1961; Dubinin, 1976). These hypotheses are, however, applied mainly to microorganisms and lower plants; for higher plants there are still not satisfactory explanations of causes that underlie the mechanisms of "maximum and decrease of mutations".

The results of our experiments on mutagenic efficiency of ethyleneimine (EI) made it possible to suggest a hypothesis that explains one of the possible causes of mutation decrease at high mutagen doses in higher plants.

In the experiment carried out air-dry seeds were soaked for 18 hours at various EI concentrations at +18*degrees*C with consequent sowing of seeds into the soil in vegetative boxes and growth of plants at the Alpine station of "Rui-Dasht". The EI efficiency was measured by the frequency of chlorophyll mutations registered with the help of the "embryo test" (Moller, 1963). Number of mutations per 100 initial cells was the index of mutagen efficiency.

Designations are the same as in the above mentioned monograph. The results are given in Table 1. Figure 1 represents "dose effect" curves plotted by the method of sliding average (Urbach, 1964).

Sharp distinction between mutation frequences of Ma and Mc deserves consideration. While Mc curve grows with the increase of mutagen dose, as it was expected, the Ma curve is characterized with strongly marked maximum at EI concentration of 0.02 - 0.04%. At low concentrations Ma values are significantly greater than corresponding Mc values and the difference becomes insignificant only at concentrations above 0.04% (Table 1).

As both Ma and Mc values serve to estimate one and the same value the observed difference shows that one of the methods of calculation is based on wrong suggestions. Among them may be the acceptance of independence of number of initial cells on mutagen dosage (Langridge, 1958; Ivanov, 1974).

By segregation data of chimeric plants we counted the number of initial cells for each EI concentration. The expected diminution of the number of initial cells with the increase of mutagen dosage (chi sq.4 = 24.6; P < 0.001) was observed. The obtained values of number of initial cells were used for a repeated calculation of mutation frequency according to formula I. Corrected values of mutation frequencies (Ma) are given in Table 1, the corresponding "dose effect" curve is in Figure 1.

Figure 1: Influence of number of initial cells on character of "dose effect" curves.

As it is shown in the figure the obtained curve rises monotonously with the increase of dose and most interestingly it does not practically differ from the Mc curve. As Mc values are not dependent on the number of initial cells closeness of Ma and Mc values shows the importance of taking into account the variation of number of initial cells after EI treatment. The detected regularity will be obviously true to other mutagenes. Therefore, for true interpretation of data in experimental mutagenesis it is necessary to take into account the possible dependence of the number of initial cells induced by mutagen dosage. Otherwise there will be observed various apparent deviations of the 'dose effect' curve from theoretic expectation which may lead to the pseudophenomenon of "maximum and decrease of mutations".

In conclusion it is necessary to point out that if the number of initial cells is 2 it is quite sufficient for exposure of chimeric plants to analyse every 5 pods per plant (Moller, 1963); at greater numbers of initial cells this amount of pods will be insufficient.
