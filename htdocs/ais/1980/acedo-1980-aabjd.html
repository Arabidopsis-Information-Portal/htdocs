THE EFFECT OF pH ON THE GROWTH OF MUTANT A-154

Gregoria N. Acedo

Department of Agronomy, University of Missouri, Columbia, Missouri 65211, U.S.A.

A mutant of Arabidopsis, A-154, was previously characterized as being sensitive to valine and other amino acids (Acedo and Redei, 1976), and the glutamic acid content was found to be low in the free amino acid pool (Acedo, 1978). Some metabolites supplied exogenously improved the growth of the mutant; among these are formate (Acedo, 1980 in this issue), malate and para-amino-benzoid acid. This paper reports on the improved growth of mutant A-154 on culture media with low pH, the possible implications of this effect are also discussed.

The plants were grown aseptically on solid media (Redei, 1962). Supplements were added and the pH was adjusted prior to autoclaving. The seeded test tubes were put in the greenhouse for 20-30 days, then the plants harvested were dried in an oven for 18-24 hours at 85*degrees*C before weighing.

Glutamic acid and casein hydrolyzate which inhibit growth of this mutant at pH between 5.5 to 6.5 are not inhibitory at pH 4.5 (Table 1). The growth of the wild type, however, is slightly retarded at low pH.

I suspect that the membrane system of the mutant is probably made more permeable to some metabolites at pH 4.5. Low pH increased the availability of iron to soybean plants (Weiss, 1943). Another possibility is that the low pH directly altered the activity of the enzymes. Lipoxidase, acid phosphatase, and *beta*amylase were among the major proteins identified at pH 4.5 on soybean cotyledons (Catsimpolas and Leuthner, 1969). A definite effect of pH on the kinetics of catalase-mediated reactions was demonstrated by chance (1952). In the 'malic' enzyme reaction, the Km for malate is dependent on pH (Johnson and Hatch, 1970). Valine inhibition of growth in Salmonella typhimurium is also dependent on pH, the greatest inhibition is at pH 8.0 (Bauerle et al., 1964).

The improvement of growth of mutant A-154 in the glutamic acid-supplemented media could be due to a decreased uptake since this amino acid is toxic at pH 6.5. With casein hydrolyzate, a selective permeability to some amino acids nontoxic for growth could have been favored and the entry of the toxic ones reduced. There is an established feedback system in this mutant especially in the valine: isoleucine pathway (Acedo and Redei, 1976) and between threonine and isoleucine (unpublished data). In both cases isoleucine relieves the toxic effects of both amino acids. The altered kinetics of some enzymes, particularly the permeases should also be considered. Many other possibilities exist, and the actual reaction affected by the low pH would be an interesting subject for future study.
