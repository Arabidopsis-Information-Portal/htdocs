RADIORESISTANCE OF NATURAL POPULATIONS OF ARABIDOPSIS THALIANA (L.) Heynh. (Pamiro-Alay)

V.A. Shevchenko and Kh. Kh. Khurmatov

Institute of General Genetics, USSR Academy of Sciences, Moscow

INTRODUCTION

In this study we are dealing with the effect of gamma irradiation on seeds of plants from natural populations of Arabidopsis thaliana growing at different altitudes. The habitat of the populations is the southern slope of Gissersky mountain ridge (Pamiro-Alay). USMANOV et al. (1978) have considered in great detail the climatic conditions of this region and the temporal characteristics of population's vegetation cycle. According to these authors, natural populations of Arabidopsis growing in Tajikistan differ markedly from one another in a number of traits and in the fraction of cross polinating plants. Populations growing at different altitudes have also been shown to differ in the relative number of winter and spring forms. The latter fact is of considerable interest for further studies of the genetic control of this character in plants (USMANOV et al., 1978; Khurmatov and Shevchenko, 1981). Cetl (1979) studied Arabidopsis populations of Khodga-Obi-Garm and Moravia and revealed certain heterogeneity between them in the onset of flowering.

MATERIAL AND METHODS

The subject of the present study were Arabidopsis seeds originating from three regions of Tajikistan: Kondara (1100 m), Khodga-Obi-Garm (1800 m) and Sorbo (2200 m above sea-level).

The seeds were studied with regard to their resistance to gamma irradiation (doses: 40, 80, 160 and 240 kr). The population studies displayed significant differences from one another in their radioresistance analysed under laboratory conditions following irradiation of air-dried seeds (moisture content 5%) at different doses. The parameters assayed were germination capacity, viability, rate of growth (length of root on 7th day) and some others.

RESULTS

As it is shown in Table 1, irradiation at 240 kr produced little effect on the germination capacity of seeds from all three populations. The exceptionally high radio resistance of A. thaliana seeds seems to be due to the small size of the chromosomes and seeds, as well as to the complete type of embryogenetic charactristic of Arabidopsis seeds (Ivanov, 1974).

We also studied dose-response reltionships for the three populations Table 1 and Figure 1 present data on germination capacity and plant survival as detrmined in the cotyledon phase of growth. Irradiation of seeds caused death of plants at very early stages. According to Ivanov (1974), this is due to radiation injury of meristematic cells in the embryos. As can be seen from Figure 1, the Khodga-Obi-Garm population (1800 m) is the most resistant by the survival criterion.

Table 2 and Figure 2 show plant growth rate for the three populations measured as the length of root 7 days after the onset of germination. The Khodga-Obi-Garm population is the most resistant by this criterion, too. The dose resulting in a 50% reduction of growth rate, as detrmined from the dose-response curves, equals 35 kr, 60 kr and 46 kr for the Kondara, Khodga-Obi-Garm and Sorbo populations, respectively. We also identified doses which induce stimulation (up to 40 kr) or inhibition (over 160 kr) of plant growth and development.

CONCLUSION

It is demonstrated that natural Arabidopsis populations growing at different altitudes differ in their radioresistance. The 1800 m-population is the most resistant one, while those inhabiting higher or lower altitudes are less resistant. Supposingly, the altitude function of radioresistance is not linear but rather has a maximum which may be due to the existence, for each plant species, of a set of optimum conditions (including, in particular, the habitat altitude) that ensures maximum radioresistance. Another contributing factor in Alpine populations may be the selection of plans particularly resistant to physical factors whose intensity increases with altitude (temperature differential, high intensity of UV and cosmic radiation). The authors see their future objective in a more detailed study of radioresistance of A. thaliana and some other plants as a function of habitat altitude, as well as in a research into the genetic nature of radioresistance differences in plants.
