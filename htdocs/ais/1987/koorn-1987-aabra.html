A MARKER LINE THAT ALLOWS THE DETECTION OF LINKAGE ON ALL ARABIDOPSIS CHROMOSOMES

M. Koornneef, C.J. Hanhart, E.P. Van Loenen Martinet and J.H. Van der Veen

Department of Genetics, Agricultural University, Generaal Foulkesweg 53, 6703 BM Wageningen, The Netherlands

The mapping of genes on a linkage map has become increasingly relevant by the development of molecular genetic procedures that allow the isolation of genes (as DNA) when they are located closely to genes for which isolated DNA is available. Arabidopsis is especially suitable for these approaches because of the small size of its genome (Meyerowitz and Pruitt, 1985). To do this, DNA markers such as restriction fragment length polymorphism (RFLP) markers have to be mapped also in relation to the existing linkage map (Koornneef et al., 1983; Koornneef, 1987). For reasons of efficiency, scoring should be done for as many markers as possible in a single population.

In this report we describe a genotype (called W100) that is homozygous recessive for ten marker genes, two located on each of the five Arabidopsis chromosomes. This genotype was constructed by combination, in a number of successive generations, of homozygous recessive mutants available from our mapping program (Koornnef and Hanart, 1983). Line W100 is in the Landsberg "erecta" genetic background and its marker genes are listed in Table 1. All are distinguishable in each others background. When grown under normal growing conditions they can be scored unambiguously. All these genes have been used extensively in the construction of the Arabidopsis linkage map, which implies that their map position (Table 1, Figure 1) is firmly established.

For locating a recessive mutant only the F 2 (repulsion) will ve available. With a dominant mutant both F 2 (coupling) and testcrosses (with the marker line as the background parent) can be used. For mutants with intermediate heterozygots (including RFLP markers) both testcross and F 2's are useful.

It can be seen from Figure 2 that recombination fractions of r<0.35 are detectable at reasonable manageable population sizes. When correcting r=0.35 for double cross overs with the Kosambi mapping function (see Koornneef et al., 1983) this results in a map distance of 43.4 cM. As linkage at this distance around the markers of W100 can be detected with a high probability it can be seen (Figure 1) that only genes located on a central part of chromosome 1 and the distal part of chromosome 3 may escape detection.

Once linkage have been detected one can estimate r from F 2 data either by maximum likelihood or by the Product Ratio Method using the tables of Stevens (1939). See e.g., Mather (1951). For also applying the Product Ratio Method in case of mutants with intermediate heterozygotes, phenotypes can be pooled to give a coupling phase F 2 which hardly reduces the accuracy of the estimate of r.
