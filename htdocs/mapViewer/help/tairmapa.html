Introduction

TAIR's comprehensive MapViewer for Arabidopsis thaliana is an integrated graphic display of each Arabidopsis chromosome. This display shows genetic, physical, and sequence maps in one site and allows users to search, browse, and align different maps in a region of interest. Please note that this is a second version of the Map Viewer and we intend to improve its functionality in future versions.

Please see "Release Update Notes" for more information about this release. If you come across any glitches in the tool as well as in the data shown here, please contact us at: curator@arabidopsis.org

You can browse chromosome maps or search for specific locations on any chromosome.

Note: The initial alignment of the different maps to each other are only APPROXIMATE. To improve the alignment, center the maps on a shared entity using the search box or the scroll left/right arrows. Remember that marker order and spacing often differ between genetic and physical/sequence maps. Please use caution in interpreting alignments between different map types.

Map Types

TAIR's maps are divided into three general types:

  • Genetic maps are sets of markers that have been mapped by crossing parents with two different forms of the marker (in the case of many classical markers, these are the wild-type and the mutant) and scoring how many recombination events separate the marker from other markers on the same map.
  • Physical maps are collections of overlapping clones that have been arranged into a tiling path based on either fingerprinting (digestion of clones with restriction enzymes and comparison of the fragment sizes) or hybridization.
  • Sequence maps are sets of clones that have been sequenced or have been chosen for sequencing by the Arabidopsis Genome Initiative (AGI).

In regions where sequencing is complete, the order and overlaps between the clones has usually been confirmed by direct sequence comparison. Many genetic markers have been located on the physical and sequence maps by hybridization or direct sequence comparison when the sequence of the marker is available. However, the order and spacing of markers is frequently different for markers that appear on both physical and genetic maps for a variety of reasons. Genetic mapping distances depend on recombination rate as well as physical distance, and the order of genetic markers can depend on the quality and quantity of the mapping data. In a physical map, the position or orientation of a contig (set of overlapping clones) on the chromosome may be incorrect.

For details about the specific maps, see "Close-Up on Sequence Maps", "Close-Up on Genetic Maps", and "Close-Up on Physical Maps".

Centromeres, Telomeres, and NORs:

The centromere is a region of the chromosome to which the spindle fibers attach during cell division. A telomere is a region at the end of a chromosome that aids in replication of chromosome ends. Both centromeres and telomeres contain DNA sequence repeats and for this reason are difficult to sequence. NORs (Nucleolar Organizing Regions) are regions containing many rDNA repeats and participate in the structure of the nucleolus. Arabidopsis has two NORs, one at the top/left of chromosome 2 and the other at the top/left of chromosome 4.
