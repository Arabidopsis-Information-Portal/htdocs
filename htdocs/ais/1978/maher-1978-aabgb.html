2, 4-D RESISTANT MUTANTS

E.P. Maher and S.J.B. Martindale

Department of Genetics, Aberdeen University, Scotland

We have previously described the isolation of a mutant (83) which has increased resistance to 2,4-dichlorophenoxy-acetic acid, an altered geotropic response of its roots and an increased rate of root growth (Maher, 1977).

This combination of characteristics raises some interesting questions. For many years it was believed that indoleacetic acid (IAA), the naturally occurring counterpart of 2, 4-D, was the regulatory molecule involved in the geotropic response of roots. This would appear to tie in well with the properties of the mutant because a lesion which, for example, decreased endogenous levels of IAA might be expected to increase 2, 4-D resistance and alter the geotropic response. However, more recently the role of IAA in root geotropism has been questioned and there is now strong evidence that the response is mediated by the inhibitor abscisic acid (ABA) which is produced in the root cap and transported to the lower half of horizontally placed roots (Wilkins, 1976).

We have tested the effects of these growth regulators on both wildtype and mutant root growth. Also included are the related herbicides, 4-chloro-2-methylphenoxy-acetic acid (MCPA) and 2, 4, 5-trichlorophenoxyacetic acid (2, 4, 5-T).

The table shows that similar levels of 2,4-D and IAA inhibit the wildtype, but the mutant has appeciable resistance to both compounds. On the other hand, there is only a marginal difference between wildtype and mutant in inhibitory concentrations of ABA. It would be of particular interest to measure the endogenous levels of these compounds in wildtype and mutant roots to see if they correlate with the data in Table 1.

A difference is also found in the effects of the three phenoxyacetic acid herbicides. While 2, 4-D and MCPA work at similar concentrations and are resisted strongly by the mutant 2, 4, 5-T requires higher concentrations to be inhibitory and the mutant only shows a 2.2 fold increase in resistance.

While the characterization of mutant 83 continues, we are also extending our range of 2,4-D resistant mutants induced with ethylmethane sulphonate. To date we have twenty-six mutants which were isolated in M2 and which displayed some level of resistance to 2, 4-D in M3. Of these the majority appear to be like 83 with similar levels of resistance and altered geotropic responses. Among the remainder are types with lower levels of resistance but apparently normal geotropic responses and one M3 family which is segregating very highly resistant seedlings.

We have also looked for naturally occurring resistance to 2, 4-D in different geographical races of Arabidopsis. We have screened over 70 races (seed originally supplied by Prof. Kranz) but have found only one, Ts-7, from Spain, which shows any appreciable difference to our standard Landsberg strain with a 1.4 fold increase in resistance.
