1. Overview

Experiments : 1. Crossing Arabidopsis

2. Staining pollen tubes

3. Staining pollen for GUS activity

The goal of this lab is to demonstrate basic techniques used in crossing Arabidopsis thaliana, as well as techniques for assessing the viability and fertility of pollen.

Schedule: Experiments 2 and 3 may require 1 - 2 hours. You may wish to initiate those experiments and then to cross your plants during the incubation steps or while waiting to use the microscopes or other equipment.

2. EXPERIMENT #1: CROSSES WITH ARABIDOPSIS THALIANA

Crosses with Arabidopsis can be quite challenging due to the small size of the flowers. The instructors will demonstrate methods for removing anthers and pollinating stigmas. The techniques require patience and practice, but after some experience, crosses require only 1-3 minutes each.

Cross the following plants:

1) Female: albino 1/+ heterozygotes

Male: Landsberg erecta

2) Female: male-sterile ms1/ms1 mutants

Male: Landsberg erecta

The albino 1 mutation is a recessive embryonic or seedling lethal and must be maintained by propagating heterozygotes. When albino1/+ plants are selfed, the developing siliques segregate 3:1 for wild-type : white (dead) seeds. If you have successfully crossed wild-type pollen onto the albino1/+ plant, you will be able to see (before the end of the course) few, if any, dead seeds.

Crossing onto a male-sterile line can be extremely convenient, minimizing the work required for emasculation. The ms1 mutation is recessive and has a severe defect in pollen development (seed kindly donated by M. Koornneef). Within 3-6 days after a successful cross, you will see seed pod expansion, and, if you remove the epidermal layer of the pistil, you will be able to see the developing seeds. Fully fertilized seed pods will contain approximately 60 seeds.

3. EXPERIMENT #2: STAINING POLLEN TUBES

A useful method for monitoring pollen fertility is to stain and visualize pollen tubes as they grow on the stigma and through the transmitting tract. In this experiment we will observe the growth of pollen tubes on self-pollinated wild-type plants.

1. Remove open flowers from wild-type plants. Newly pollinated flowers are best to use, but you can also look at flowers with elongating siliques. Before placing the pistils in fixative, remove the petals, and (if you wish) the anthers, as these will often obscure the view of the pistil.

2. Fix the pistils for at least 1.5 hours (can go overnight) in 1 ml of:

10% acetic acid

30% chloroform

60% ethanol

3. Pipet off fixative and suspend in 1 ml 4N NaOH for 10 min.

4. Neutralize with 3 washes of 50 mM potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.5).

5. Move pistils to a slide and add a drop of 0.05% aniline blue in 50 mM phosphate buffer (pH 7.5) Gently apply a cover slip and view pollen tubes on the fluorescent microscope (use the DAPI filter). To see the tubes on the interior of the pistil, you will need to squash the tissue firmly with the cover slip. With this stain, pollen grains (but not pollen tubes) also can be visualized with the rhodamine filter.

4. EXPERIMENT #3: STAINING POLLEN FOR GUS ACTIVITY

4.1 Introduction

A useful method for studying pollen gene expression is to analyze the levels of a reporter enzyme such as ß-glucuronidase (GUS) under the control of a promoter of interest. Here we present a simple method for examining GUS expression in pollen.

Transgenic plants that contain a fusion of the LAT52 promoter (Twell, et al., 1989, Mol. Gen. Genet. 217: 240; and Twell et al., 1990, Development 109: 705) to ß-glucuronidase will be provided. The LAT52 promoter is a well-characterized pollen-specific promoter from tomato that is active in many plant species, including Arabidopsis. Because transcription from this promoter occurs only after meiosis, plants heterozygous for a LAT52-GUS transgene will produce both GUS-positive and GUS-negative pollen grains in a 1:1 ratio.

The transgenic plants also contain a mutation in the quartet1 gene (qrt1, Preuss et al., 1994, Science, 264:1458-1460). This mutation causes the four products of pollen meiosis to be released in an intact tetrad, rather than as individual pollen grains. Thus, in the qrt1 background, plants heterozygous for the GUS transgene will produce pollen tetrads with 2 GUS-positive and 2 GUS-negative pollen grains.

Inviable pollen grains or pollen that failed to develop properly will form little GUS protein and will stain poorly. Some of the strains provided may have low pollen viability because they were backcrossed only once following transformation. This experiment will demonstrate the effects of mutagenic procedures, such as transformation, on pollen viability.

4.2 Procedure

1. Place flowers from plants to be tested in a microfuge tube.

2. Cover the flowers with 90% acetone, and vortex to dislodge the pollen. Place a drop of the pollen suspension on a glass slide, and let the solution evaporate completely at room temperature. Multiwell slides provide a convenient means to analyze pollen from several plants on one slide.

3. Add a drop of 50 mM sodium phosphate, pH 7.0, 5 mM potassium ferrocyanide, 5 mM potassium ferricyanide, 0.5 mg/ml X-gluc (from Clontech) [5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl glucuronide]. Place slide in a humid chamber (a plastic box with a lid that contains wet paper towels) and incubate for at 0.5 - 1 hour at 37°C.

4. Assess pollen staining in the light microscope. Determine whether the plant(s) you scored are heterozygous or homozygous for the GUS transgene.
