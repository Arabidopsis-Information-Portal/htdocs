EXPERIMENTAL PROTOCOLS

Preparing DNA From Pools of Transgenic Plant Lines

T-DNA transformed Arabidopsis thaliana (ecotype Wassilewskija) populations were generated by the seed transformation protocol and most of these lines have been described previously (Forsthoefel et al., 1992) . Two different methods have been used to prepare the plant populations.

Method #1--Growing the plant population in liquid

1. Forty seeds (T3 or T4) were aliquoted from each of 5 transformed lines and pooled. The pooled seeds were surface sterilized (8 min 50%/Clorox bleach) and rinsed 5X with sterilized distilled water.

2. Each sterilized seed pool was transferred to a 250 ml flask containing 100 ml of M. & S. (Gibco BRL) supplemented with 0.5% sucrose (w/v) (pH = 6.0 before autoclaving).

3. Following a 24 hr cold treatment (4°C), the flasks containing the seeds were transferred to a shaker (30 rpm) and grown for 15 days under continuous florescent light (35 µmol s-1 m-2) at 23-25°C.

4. Approximately 5 g of tissue were harvested from each pool, blotted dry, frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at -70°C.

5. DNA was extracted from each of two pools of 5 lines (in the text, termed "a subpool of 10 lines").

Method #2--Growing the plant population in flats

1. One thousand seeds from a pool of 100 plant lines were sowed directly into 1 flat (21" x 10.5" x 2.5") of fine soil mix (Fafard 3B). The seeds were mixed with coarse sand in a small salt shaker and dispersed evenly across the flat.

2. Seeds were grown at 23°C under florescent lights (Growlux) on a cycle of 8 hr light x 16 hr darkness.

3. The flats were misted 2X a day for the first 10 days and then once a day until harvest .

4. Three-week-old seedlings were harvested by cutting off all above-ground parts and freezing immeditately in liquid nitrogen and grinding with a morter and pestle. The frozen powdered tissue was stored at -70°C in 10 g batches.

DNA extraction (McKinney et al., 1995)

1. DNA was prepared from 5-10 g of frozen tissue (prepared by either method above) using a modified CTAB (cetyltrimethylammonium bromide) DNA extraction procedure (Murray and Thompson, 1980). If not already done (i.e., Method #1), the frozen tissue was ground to a fine powder and frozen in a chilled mortar.

2. Ten ml of extraction buffer (2% CTAB, 100 mM Tris, 20 mM EDTA, 1.4 M NaCl [pH 8.0]) were added to the powder and the mix was incubated for 10 min at 60°C.

3. Ten ml of chloroform/isoamyl alcohol (24:1) were added to the incubated mix and vortexed for 10 sec. The phases were separated by centrifuge (1,500 x g).

4. The aqueous phase was transferred to a clean tube and extracted with chloroform/ isoamylalcohol for a second time.

5. After centrifuging, the aqueous phase was transferred to a clean tube and 1 ml of 10% (w/v) CTAB was added.

6. The solution was heated for 2 min at 60°C and CTAB/DNA precipitation was induced by adding 20 ml of deionized water and placing the tubes on ice from 1 hr to overnight. Rushing this step or insufficient chilling causes a major loss of yield.

7. The precipitates were collected by centrifuge and dissolved by adding 5 mls of 1 N NaCl and heating for 2 min at 60°C.

8. DNA was precipitated by adding 10 mls of 95% EtOH and was collected by centrifuge. The pellet was washed 2X, gently but thoroughly, with 5 mls 70% EtOH and air dried.

9. The dried DNA pellet from 10 g of tissue was resuspended in 200 µl of TE.

10. The DNA was quantified after visualizing an aliquot from each sample (after RNAase A treatment) on an ethidium bromide-stained agarose gel and comparing the high molecular weight band with a standard. Alternatively, micro fluorimetry (Hoefer TKO 100 mini-fluorometer) and Hoechst dye 33258 (final concentration 0.1 µg/ml) were used to quantify a approximately 10 ng sample of DNA. The typical yield was 40-250 ng of DNA per ml (8-50) µg per 10 g of tissue).

11. The pools of 100 lines were generated by mixing equal amounts of DNA from each of 10 subpools of 10 lines (Plant Population Method #1).

DNA for Rapid-Screen Prepared From Individual Transgenic Plant Lines

(McKinney et al., 1995)

1. DNA samples from individual plant lines were prepared as follows: One very young leaf 0.5 cm wide was ground briefly with a stainless steel rod in a 1.5 ml eppendorf tube, and then ground further with 100 µl of extraction buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl pH = 8.0 at 22°C, 200 mM NaCl, 0.2 mM EDTA, 0.5% sodium dodecyl sulfate, 100 µg/ml Proteinase K) and incubated at 37°C for 30 min.

2. The leaf sample was then extracted 1X with 100 µl of buffer-saturated phenol and 1X with 100 µl chloroform at room temperature.

3. The aqueous solution was made 300 mM in sodium acetate (3M stock) and the nucleic acids were precipitated with 2 volumes of ethanol.

4. The ethanol pellet was washed gently 2X with 70% ethanol and resuspended in 100 µl TE (10 mM Tris-HCl pH=8.0 22°C, 1 mM EDTA).

5. A small portion of the nucleic acid preparation, 0.5-1 µl, was used for PCR. Using more plant material or more of the final product from the purification may inhibit the PCR reaction.

T-DNA and Actin Primers (McKinney et al., 1995)

1. The actin PCR primers presented in Table I (McKinney et al., 1995) were synthesized at the University of Georgia's Molecular Genetics Instrument Facility (not purifed) and were already in use in our laboratory as DNA sequencing, mapping, and cloning primers and were not designed for these experiments. This accounts for the extreme variation in length, degeneracy, and percent GC composition. Among the sequences of a degenerate primer is a sequence identical to each of the 10 Arabidopsis actin genes, with one exception. The 284A primer contains several mismatched nucleotides relative to ACT9 and probably will not amplify this pseudogene.

2. Actin Primer Design and Controls: In reconstruction PCR experiments, the two primers used during the initial screening, 119S and 284A, where each used in conjunction with either a T7 or Sp6 vector primer and 10 ng of genomic subclones of ACT2in Bluescript SKII+ (Promega).

a. Using the PCR conditions described herein, each primer pair (i.e., Sp6/284A) could PCR amplify visually detectable levels (~50 ng) of 5-6 kb genomic/vector fragments.

b. Neither actin primer generated a single strong random amplified polymorphic DNA (RAPD) band* when used alone to amplify products from Arabidopsis DNA. This was taken as evidence that the actin primers and amplification conditions were efficient enough to detect insertions within a few kbp of the primers.

3. T-DNA primer design: The T-DNA LB (LB102A) and RB (RB16843S) primers (Table I) were synthesized based on the sequence near-ends of T-DNA element as presented in Genbank (submitted & TiPost37.ba, respectively).

a. We determined that each of these T-DNA or actin primers did not generate a strong RAPD band(s) when used alone with Arabidopsis DNA.

b. Other variables, such as locating the T-DNA primers near the border sequences, are discussed in the text. It should also be noted that the highest yield of PCR is generally for products of 200 to 1,000 bp. We find the molar yield of products less than 200 bp drops off rapidly with decreasing size. This is another advantage of keeping the T-DNA primers near the border junctions.

*If a primer generates a few weak RAPD bands when used alone against genomic DNA, these bands will not be present when a real PCR product is generated. In the past ,when primers have given us problem and generated a strong dominant RAPD band, we have successfully eliminated the problem by changing the 3' terminal 3-5 nt. In a coding region, this is done either by adding or deleting an additional codon to the 3' end of the original sequence. In the original primer design, making longer primers (26-31 mers) has been more successful at increasing product yield and decreasing background then finding primer sites with very low degeneracy. The 3' ends of primers were designed to avoid any potential to prime internally on themselves and make primer dimers (no inverse complement to the 3' end of more than 2-3 bp).

PCR Amplification and Detection of T-DNA/actin Junction Sequences

(McKinney et al., 1995)

1. PCR was performed in 50 µl reactions topped with mineral oil and containing 0.02-0.05 µg of the DNA pool in Promega's buffer, 1 unit of Taq enzyme, 5 µg RNAaseA, 25 pmoles RB or LB oligos, and 50 pmoles of the degenerate (or 25 pmoles of unique) actin primers and 0.1 mM dNTPs.

2. The protocol started with an initial 2 min incubation at 94°C, followed by 45 cycles of PCR (94°C/30 sec, 42°C/30 sec, 68°C/3 min). When unique primers are used, we generally have less background and fewer RAPDs if the annealing temperature (42°C) is raised to 52-56°C.

3. 10 µl of the product (mixed with 5 µl of sample loading mix) was run on a 1% agarose gel 0.5 x 17 x 17 cm with 2x 27 well combs stacked one above the other 8 cm apart. The gel is run at 150V for 1.5 hr, stained with ethidium bromide, and then photographed with a ruler next to the gel for size reference (or mark standards with india ink).

Southern imprint

1. The DNA samples in the 1% agarose gel were:

a) depurinated in 0.25M HCl 2 x 15 min each;

b) rinsed in distilled water 3X;

c) denatured in 0.5M NaOH, 1.5M NaCl: 20-40 min; and

d) neutralized in 3M NaCl, 0.5M Tris pH 7.0: 2 x 20 min.

2. The transfer membrane Biotrans plus (0.45µM) (ICN Biomedicals) was cut to the dimensions of the gel, wet in distilled water, and then soaked in 20XSSC until ready for transfer.

3. The DNA was vacuum-transferred using the Trans-vac TE80 (Hoefer, Inc.), which takes only 60 min, or by the following overnight method:

a. Whatman 3mm paper to be used as a wick was cut slightly wider (1/2") and longer (5") than the gel. The ends of the wick were suspended into buffer from a piece of glass or plastic that was set on top of a baking dish. The wick was wetted and placed in contact with the 20XSSC in the baking dish reservoir. The gel was then placed on top of the wick, avoiding air bubbles.

b. 20 X SSC was poured onto the gel and the Biotrans placed on top (avoiding air bubbles between gel and filter).

c. Two pieces of Whatman 3MM, wetted in 20XSSC, were cut in the same dimensions as the gel and placed on top of the membrane.

d. Four to five inches of paper towels (slightly smaller than the Whatman paper) were placed carefully on top of the gel-membrane-filter paper sandwich.

e. A piece of plastic or glass was placed on top of the paper towels and topped with a small weight.

f. The DNA transfer was allowed to proceed overnight. The stack was dissembled and the wells were marked with a nitrocellulose marking pen (S & S).

4. After either transfer method, the Biotrans membrane was remove from gel and the membrane exposed, nucleic acid side up, to strong UV light (6 ") for 2 min. The membrane was then baked for 2 hr in a 60-80°C oven. The membrane is now ready for hybridization.

5. As a probe, full-length ACT2 cDNA (An et al., 1996) was PCR amplified with Sp6 and T7 primers from a pcDNAII (Invitrogen) clone and labeled with 32P-ATP by the random primer method to a specific activity of ~300 x 106/µg.

6. The DNA containing filters were prehybridized for 4.5 hrs at 40°C in 35% formamide, 6x SSC, 5x Denhardt's (Maniatis et al., 1989) , 0.5% SDS, 25mM sodium phosphate pH 6.5, 50 µg/ml tRNA (Sigma), and 0.1% gelatin, hybridized with probe for 2 days at 40°C in the prehybridization mix

7. Filters were washed 4X at 50-56°C for 10 min, 3x SSC, 0.2% sodium dodecyl sulfate (McDowell et al., 1996) .

8. The filters were typically exposed to x-ray film with one amplification screen for 30 min or as indicated in the text.

Sequences of actin/T-DNA Junctions (McKinney et al., 1995)

1. The actin/T-DNA junctions at both ends of the insertions in act2-1 and act4-1 were PCR amplified (100µl reactions) using gene-specific or degenerate upstream or downstream primers (Table I) and the appropriate T-DNA border primer. Sometimes a series of nested PCR reactions were used to increase the PCR yield. First reaction with an outer set of oligos often yields a product so weak it cannot be visualized on a gel. This reaction was then isopropanol precipitated and resuspended in 5-20 µl water. Then 2-5 µl of this product was used as template for a PCR reaction using an inner set of oligos.

2. The PCR product was phenol extracted and precipitated with isopropanol.

3. This product was subjected to cycle sequencing (fmol DNA Sequencing System, Promega, Inc.), priming the reaction with a 32P end-labeled T-DNA border, actin gene-specific, or actin degenerate oligonucleotide.
