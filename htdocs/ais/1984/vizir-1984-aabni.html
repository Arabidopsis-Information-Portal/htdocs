ON THE FREQUENCY OF RECOMBINATION VARIABILITY IN THE SECOND CHROMOSOME OF ARABIDOPSIS THALIANA (L.) Heynh.

I. Yu Vizir

Recombinogenesis Laboratory, Department of Plant Genetics, The Academy of Sciences of the Moldavian SSR, 277028 Kishinev, USSR

INTRODUCTION

The elimination of possible artefacts in studies of recombinogenesis constitutes a serious problem. Such defects are thought to be due to noncontrolled factors (Butler, 1977).

Estimation of the variability of recombination fequency is a prerequisite for sample size optimization in F 1 and F 2 generations to eliminate noncontrolled factor effects. This article deals with the rf variability on chromosome 2 of Arabidopsis thaliana.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

The F1 fresh harvested seeds from as'er x an'ap'ch-c (a), as'hy x py'gl'an'cer'er (b) and py'gl'an'cer'er x as'hy (c) crosses were used in our tests. For the phenotypes of marker genes see Koornneef's (1983) gene mapping paper. The breeding procedure and seed harvesting were done simultaneously for groups (b) and (c), and separately for group (a). Sampling of F 2 seeds from each F 1 plant was random and corresponded to the number of seeds in 4 to 6 siliquaes.

Seeds were sown on Petri dishes containing solution-soaked (KNO 3, 0.1%) filter paper and germinated following the synchronization technique (Petrov et al., 1982). Plants were grown in soil culture under continuous fluorescent light (E = 7000 lux) at 22 to 25*degrees*C.

Environmental conditions for various F 2 populations (Table 1) were different: (I) harvested seeds were kept under room conditions and sown after two weeks, (II) harvested seeds were kept under greenhouse conditions (high levels of temperature and humidity) and sown after about a month; for the normal development of py-/py- genotypes, F2 plants were sprayed with thiamine HC1 (5 mg/1) every day.

Standard errors for rf values of individual families as well as pooled samples over the families were estimated using the formula (1) (Bailey, 1961), and formula (2) for weighted pooled values (Kendall and Stuart, 1966; Urbakh, 1964).

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

Comparisons of rf values by t-test showed the absence of differences among families as well as between F 1 reciprocal variants.

The differences between rf standard errors estimated by formulas (1) and (2) were negligible (Table 1). This indicated a low level of nonctontrolled factor effects in our experiments.

The deviation from the 3:1 ratio towards an increase of mutant plant number (1.93 for as marker, 2.87 for er marker) in the third family, probably, also accounted for a considerable degree of deviation in the sum value (2.70 for as marker, 2.87 for er marker). This is due not only to the selective elimination of F2 plants, since the observed death rate was low (Table 1). However, it can be assumed that this result from selection of wild type gametes (Zhuchenko, 1980).

The correlation between death rate and rf in F 2 conditions (II) is -0.746, though it should be verified for a larger F 2 population size. Yet, the deviation from the 3:1 ratio towards an increase in dominant plant number of individual F 2 families points to inverse correlation.

The t-test comparisons of our rf values for as'er and as'hy segments with those of Koornneef (1983), weighted by us using formula (2) showed the absence of differences (16.20 +/- 1.39 and 18.07 +/- 1.96 for as'er segment, 13,75 +/- 1.90 and 15.83 +/- 1.14 for the as'hy segment, respectively).

CONCLUSION

1. Variability of rf among F 1 plants can be attributed to statistical reasons.

2. The correlation between the rf values and F 2 viability is weak although is stronger for individual F 2 families.

3. The effet of reciprocal crossing on the rf values in the as'hy region is absent.

4. The rf value is fairly stable under growth conditions close to optimum.
