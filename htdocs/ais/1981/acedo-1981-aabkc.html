ACTIVITIES OF SOME ENZYMES IN ARABIDOPSIS

Gregoria N. Acedo

Department of Agronomy, University of Missouri Columbia, Missouri 65211, U.S.A.

INTRODUCTION

Mutant A-154 of Arabidopsis overproduces some of the oxidases especially tyrosinase and peroxidase wherease it is low in some of the ammonia-assimilating enzymes (Acedo, 1981). The activities of other enzymes were investigated in an effort to locate the metabolic block in mutant A-154.

The levels of adenosine triphosphate (ATP'ase), ribulose biphosphate carboxylase (RUBP'case), and acid phosphatase in the mutant and in the wild type are compared.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

The plants were grown in Pro-Mix soil (Premier Brands Inc., New York, N.YU. 10017) 3-inch deep petri plates. The plates were kept in a growth chamber illuminated with approximately 1000 footcandles and the temperature was maintained at 24*degrees*C. After growth for 20 days, the plants were harvested, washed thoroughly and used for the enzyme assays described below.

Adenosine Triphosphatase: The enzyme was assayed according to Henry and Richard (1978). The assay mixture consists of 1.9 ml medium (50 micro M Tris buffer, pH 8.0, 5 micro M MgCl 2, 10 micro M dithiothreitol (DTT), 0.05 micro M phenazine methosulfate (PMS), and 100 micro M sucrose); 0.3 ml of 5 micro M ATP, and 0.5 ml enzyme extract. The wavelength was read at 420 nm against a standard without the enzyme extract.

Ribulose Biphosphate Carboxylase: The assay procedure used for RUBP'case is a light modification of the method of Randall et al. (1977). The plants were homogenized in 0.1M Tricine, pH 8.0, 2mM DTT, 5mM MgCl2, and 1 percent Polyclar AT. The homogenate was centrifuged at 30.000 g for 10 minutes and afterwards dialyzed for 2 hours in a media similar in composition to the extraction medium but without the Polyclar AT. The assay mixture contained 50 micro l of a mixture of 0.2M Tris, pH 8.1, 10mM, New England Nuclear), and 40 micro l enzyme extract. The reaction mixture was pre-equilibrated at 25*degrees*C before addition of 10 micro l of 1 mM RUBP. Aliquots of 50 micro l were removed at 30, 60 and 120 seconds and put in a vial with 3 volumes of 3N HCl-methanol. The samples were dried and the residue was redissolved in 50 micro l NaHCO 3, then 50 micro l of 3 N HCl-methanol was added and the samples dried again. The residue was redissolved in water and 4 ml of scintillation solution (1 part Triton X-100, 2 parts toluene scintillation solution composed of 8.25 g PPO and 0.25 g M 2 POPOP per liter toluene) was added. Radioactivity was counted in a Packard scintillation counter.

The extraction medium for acid phosphatase consisted of 100 mM Tris-acetate, pH 5.5, with 1.5 percent Polyclar AT. The reaction mixture according to Rhoades and Stewart (1976), contained 10 micro M Tris-acetate, pH 5.5 and 4 micro M p-nitrophenyl phosphate pe-equilibrated at 30*degrees*C, then 0.5 ml of the enzyme extract was added to a final volume of 2 ml. The reaction was stopped with 1 ml N NaOH after 15 minutes and the absorbance read at 405 nm.

ATP'ase is about 2x more in mutant A-154 than in the wild type (Table 1). Like tyrosinase and peroxidase (submitted for publication) ATP'ase is also overproduced in the mutant. One important role of ATP'ase in the cells is to maintain the mebrane in an energized state so as to promote the operation of the active transport systems (Henry and Richard, 1978), and as a terminal step in the production of ATP. The abnormally high amount of ATP'ase could contribute to the anomaly in membrane function in mutant A-154. The beneficial effect of low pH (Acedo, 1980) and the increased uptake of some metabolites by the mutant over the wild type (results to be published elsewhere) are further evidences that the membrane system in this mutant is defective.

The activity of RUBP'case in the mutant is slightly lower than in the wild type. This enzyme, being the most abundant protein in the cells, is not expected to be totally absent in the mutant, otherwise there would be no photosynthesis, however, it was of interest to know the level of this enzyme in the mutant. The low activity of RUBP'case may contribute to the comparatively slow growth of mutant A-154.

Acid phosphatase activity is lower in the mutant than in the wild type. This enzyme is involved in the mobilization of nutrient reserves (Baker and Takeo, 1973) and in the transport of sugars and other metabolites across the membranes (Flinn and Smith, 1967), and in the differentiation of plastids (Mlodzianowski, 1971)

Like the oxidases and the ammonia-assimilating enzymes studied earlier, it is apparent that the activities of adenosine triphosphatase, ribulose biphosphate carboxylase, and acid phosphatase are under the control of a common regulatory gene and the main genetic lesion is still the subject of continuing investigation.
