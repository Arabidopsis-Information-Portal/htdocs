GERMINATION RESPONSES TO SOME PROMOTING FACTORS IN DIFFERENT GENOTYPES OF ARABIDOPSIS THALIANA

Nobuharu Goto

Department of Biology, Miyagi College of Education, Aoba-Yama, Sendai 980, Japan

INTRODUCTION

In general, seeds of Arabidopsis thaliana require light for the induction of germination. The germination sensitivity for light changes with genotypes as well as with the time of after-ripening and the light conditions of parent plants (McCullough and Shropshire, 1970). On the other hand, the induction of germination is promoted by such substances as gibberellins, ethylene and KNO 3 (Spruit et al., 980; Goto, 1982). Thus I have surveyed the germination responses of seeds of a number of genotypes to brief exposure of red light and the substances promoting the induction of germination.

METHODS

Seeds of various mutant strains of Arabidopsis thaliana were harvested from plants grown in a greenhouse in spring 1982 and stored in a plastic desiccator in darkness at room temperature until germination tests were made (from January to March 1983). The seeds in lots of 500 were sown on 0.8% agar (20 ml) in petri dishes (6 cm in diameter) and allowed to germinate for 5 days at 25*degrees*C in a moist container in the dark. Red light (a 20 W red fluorescent lamp, max. ca. 660 nm 100 micro W/cm 2 at seed level) was irradiated for 10 minutes after 24 hrs incubation, and the germination percentages were determined after additonal incubation in the dark at 25*degrees*C for 4 days. Ga 3 and Ga 7 (10E-4 M), pH 6.0 with 0.05 M KOH) and KNO 3 (10E-2 M) were included in the ager medium. Ethulene (1 ml) was introduced into a 6 liter acryl box in which the dishes with seeds were placed.

RESULTS

Table 1 shows the germination responses of 19 lines and mutant strains to the factors. Red light induced germination of only several strains, but more prolonged red irradiation caused germination in many strains (data not presented). GA 7 promoted the germination of almost all stains except for line Enkheim and its promoting activity was higher than that of GA3. The effects of KNO 3 and ethylene varied with strains and were generally lower than those of GAs. In adition, KNO 3 promoted the hypocotyl elongation of the seedlings in the dark (data not presented). Line Landsberg responded to all the tested factors, and germinated even in the untreated dark control althouth its extent was low. Line Enkhiem showed no significant response to the factors tested, and required relatively high intensity and prolonged irradition of white light for germination. Strain g responded to KNO 3 and GA 7, but not to red light, GA 3 and ethylene. Strain Ng-5 that was gibberellin-deficient mutant (Koornneef et al. 1977) requiring GAs for germination responded partially to ethylene. The averages of the strains indicated the promoting effect for germination being in order GA 7, GA 3, KNO 3, red light, ethylene.

Auxin (IAA, 2, 4-D), cytokinin (kinetin, benzyladenine) and abscisic acid had no promoting effect for dark germination in either strain.
