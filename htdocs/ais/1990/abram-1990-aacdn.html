GENETIC CONSEQUENCES OF RADIOACTIVE CONTAMINATION OF ARABIDOPSIS POPULATIONS.

V.I. ABRAMOV, O.M. FEDORENKO and V.A. SHEVCHENKO

V.I. Vavilov-Institute of General Genetics, USSR Academy of Sciences, Gubkin Str.3, 117 809 Moscow B-333, USSR

Received and accepted Dec. 04, 1989

To estimate genetic consequences of radioactive contamination of plant and animal populations, it is necessary to study the dynamic process of mutation in chronically irradiated populations. It is necessary to know the temporal accumulation of genetic load in chronically irradiated populations per generation, to study the relationship of the genetic load to the level of radioactive contamination. Adaptability of different plant and animal species to the radiation factor should be determined as well. All these problems are the subjects of the research project started by us in 1986 in the 30km zone of Chernobyl Nuclear Power Station (CNPS). One of the bio-objects chosen for investigation is Arabidopsis thaliana found in abundance on the contaminated territory. It should be noted that the natural populations of Arabidopsis in the area of the accident are represented by a mixture of winter and spring forms, which impedes a little their studies under the laboratory conditions. On the whole, any mutation decreasing the fitness of their carriers may be considered as a genetic load. We have used the embryo test to study the total frequency of embryo lethal mutations and the frequency of those chlorophyll mutations which become also lethal in the homozygote state. The frequency of embryo lethals and chlorophyll mutations was analysed in the generation of field material. To its end, the main flower-bearing stems were cut off from plants growing on experimental plots, brought to the laboratory and used for analysis. The lower mature pods were collected for subsequent verification of the results of analysis, the upper younger pods were examined with a binocular lens to detect lethal embryos and embryos with chlorophyll mutations. Three parameters of the mutation process are used in studying induced mutagenesis by the embryo test: frequency of M1 plants producing segregating offsprings (Ma), frequency of fruits (pods) containing mutations on M1 plants (Mb), and frequency of mutants segregating in M2 (Mc); >50 plants per population and 3-5 pods per plant were analysed.

The relationship between the embryo lethal frequency and the dose rate of chronic irradiation presented in Table 1 can be approximated best of all by a power function. In particular, this relationship for Ma, Mb, Mc, respectively, takes the following forms:

yMa = 21,54x 0,25, yMb = 11,59x 0,28, yMc = 2,225x 0,30

where x is the dose rate.

Analysis of the regression equations obtained indicates that the temporal growth of chronic irradiation dose rate exceeds the temporal growth of mutagenic efficiency of radiation, which is seen from the values of power indexes: 0,25, 0,28 and 0,30. Consequently, at comparatively low dose rates the efficiency of ionizing radiation per dose or dose rate unit is higher than at higher dose rates. In earlier studies (SHEVCHENKO and POMERANCEVA, 1985), an interpretation of such dose response relationship was presented. Monitoring of the mutation load dynamics in successive generations may permit the specific features of accumulation or elimination of the genetic load from the Arabidopsis populations.
