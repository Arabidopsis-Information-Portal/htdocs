CELL CULTURE OF ARABIDOPSIS THALIANA ON DIFFERENT NITROGEN SOURCES

H.J. Scholten

Department of Genetics, Biology Center, University of Groningen, Kerklaan 30, 9751 NN Haren, The Netherlands

Nitrate reductase negative mutants of Nicotiana tabacum have been isolated in cell cultures by Moller and Grafe (1978) using a chlorate-containing medium with amino acids as nitrogen source. The mutant cells are unable to grow on a standard medium containing nitrate as sole nitrogen source or on media with ammonium, with or without nitrate. When a low level of enzyme activity is left the mutant cells can be cultured on a medium with ammonium as nitrogen source. In the investigations reported here the growth of cells of A. thaliana on a medium with nitrate and ammonium (PG2; Negrutiu et al., 1975) was compared with growth on other nitrogen sources as part of an attempt to isolate nitrate reductase-less mutants of A. thaliana by selection for chlorate resistance in cell cultures. These investigations were stimulated by the observation that in cultures of A. thaliana kept in the dark the concentration of the mutagenic nitrite was three times as high as in cultures kept in light. Since most cells are cultured in dark other nitrogen sources than nitrate might be desirable for a stable culture.

In the table presented the results are given of some growth experiments of wildtype and a nitrate reductase-less mutant of A. thaliana isolated in plant stage (B25; Oostindier-Braaksma and Feenstra, 1974).

As can be seen in Table 1, growth of wild type cells on a medium with amino acids as nitrogen source is comparable to that of medium with nitrate and ammonium. B25-cells exhibit a low level of enzyme activity and therefore may be expected to use ammonium as nitrogen source without being effected by ammonium toxicity. As expected B25 shows an increase in growth on a medium with amino acids.

Amino acids as nitrogen source also appear to have a positive effect on the sensitivity of cells of A. thaliana for chlorate. The first results indicate that cells cultured on PG11 are sensitive to chlorate concentrations above 100 ppm. whereas cells grown on PG2 show no effect of chlorate with concentrations up to 500 ppm. This increased sensitivity to chlorate can shorten the period of selection and prevent damage by chlorate without reduction to chlorite but due to accumulation in the cells to toxic concentrations.

From the results reported here it was concluded that when selection for chlorate resistance is used to isolate nitrate reductase-less mutants of A. thaliana in cell cultures use of a medium with amino acids as nitrogen source gives sufficient growth of the cultures with an increased sensitivity to chlorate and avoids ammonium toxicity.
